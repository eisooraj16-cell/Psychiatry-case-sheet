A1 NIMHANS Framework — The 4-Axis System
▾

Why this matters

The 4-axis discipline separates clinical syndrome from personality, physical illness, and psychosocial context. The line ordering is sacred: Axis I → II → III → IV. In KMCRI viva, candidates must write all four axes line by line without prompting.

  • Multiple Axis I diagnoses are listed in order of clinical priority (the diagnosis driving the current presentation first).
  • Axis II must NOT be diagnosed during an acute Axis I episode — the state-versus-trait trap is among the highest-yield viva failures.
  • Axis III forces the organic question; Axis IV prevents diagnosis from becoming a decontextualised label.

Axis I — Clinical Syndromes

Sample phrasing: "Bipolar affective disorder type I, current episode manic with mood-congruent psychotic features, severe, multiple prior episodes (ICD-11: 6A60.4 / ICD-10: F31.2)". A complete Axis I line includes: disorder name + course specifier + severity + current state + psychotic features if present + ICD-11 code + ICD-10 code if needed.
List additional diagnosable mental disorders. Common pairings: substance use disorder + mood disorder; anxiety disorder + depression; OCD + tics; PTSD + substance use.

Axis II — Personality & Developmental

ICD-11 PD format: "Personality disorder, [mild/moderate/severe], with prominent features of [trait domains], [with/without] borderline pattern qualifier (ICD-11: 6D11.5 / 6D10.0)". Trait domains: Negative affectivity, Detachment, Dissociality, Disinhibition, Anankastia. Exam trap: Do not diagnose PD during an acute Axis I episode. Defer until state symptoms have settled.
Explain whether the acute episode obscures trait assessment. Document your plan to re-evaluate.

Axis III — Physical Illness

Always include: conditions that contribute to presentation, restrict drug choice, or are themselves contributory. Common high-yield omissions: hypothyroidism, B12 deficiency, autoimmune encephalitis, frontal tumour, post-stroke mood disorder, hepatic impairment, pregnancy/lactation.

Axis IV — Psychosocial & Environmental

DSM-IV-TR Axis IV categories: Problems with primary support group; social environment; education; occupation; housing; economic; healthcare access; legal; other psychosocial. Indian context additions: joint-family dynamics, dowry, caste-related stress, migration, expressed-emotion environment.

Axis V — Functional Impairment (Optional / Folded into Axis IV)

GAF (legacy DSM-IV): 0–100 scale. WHODAS 2.0 (current): 0–100 disability score across 6 domains. State the functional impairment regardless of where you put it.
A2 Why Diagnosis Matters — The Probabilistic Stance
▾

Why this matters diagnostically

Diagnosis is probabilistic, not categorical. The examiner is listening for diagnostic humility alongside diagnostic commitment. A flat statement of one diagnosis without acknowledging the differential signals over-confidence and is marked down.

Template: "The working diagnosis is [primary], with [differential 1] and [differential 2] on the differential, ranked in that order. The discriminating features are [feature A favouring primary over D1], [feature B favouring primary over D2], and the single most decisive feature in this patient is [feature]."
B1 ICD-11 vs ICD-10 vs DSM-5-TR — The Big Picture
▾

How this shifts the differential

Classification system choice matters in India. ICD-11 is the official Indian classification (notified 2022). ICD-10 remains in legal use for disability certification and insurance. DSM-5-TR is the research standard. You must be able to read all three and primarily write in ICD-11.

B2 High-Yield ICD-11 Reforms — Moderate Depth
▾

What to ask and how to document

Five examinable reforms: (1) Schizophrenia subtypes abolished → symptomatic specifiers; (2) PD revolution → severity + trait domains + borderline qualifier; (3) CPTSD as distinct from PTSD; (4) OCRD grouping; (5) ASD single diagnosis with severity grading. Document which apply to your patient and how they change the diagnostic line.

C1 Schizophrenia / First-Episode Psychosis
▾

Diagnostic documentation checklist

ICD-11 (6A20): ≥2 of core symptoms for ≥1 month, with ≥1 from first four (delusions, hallucinations, thought disorder, passivity). Document symptomatic specifiers, course specifiers, and duration carefully. DSM-5-TR requires ≥6 months total disturbance.

  • Pitfall: Brief psychotic disorder is <1 month; schizophreniform is 1–6 months (DSM only); schizophrenia is ≥1 month (ICD) or ≥6 months (DSM).
  • Pitfall: Do not use abolished ICD-10 subtypes (paranoid, hebephrenic, catatonic) when writing in ICD-11.
C2 Mania / Bipolar I Disorder
▾

Mnemonic: DIGFAST

Distractibility, Indiscretion/Impulsivity, Grandiosity, Flight of ideas, Activity increase, Sleep deficit, Talkativeness. Criterion B requires ≥3 symptoms (≥4 if mood only irritable).

  • Duration ≥1 week (or any duration if hospitalised).
  • Mixed features specifier replaces the old "mixed episode" — document symptoms of opposite polarity.
  • Rapid cycling: ≥4 mood episodes per year.
C3 Bipolar Depression (BD-I or BD-II Depressive Episode)
▾

High-yield differential: BD depression vs unipolar MDD

Features favouring bipolar depression: age of onset <25, multiple prior episodes, atypical features (hypersomnia, hyperphagia, leaden paralysis), psychomotor retardation, psychotic features, postpartum onset, family history of BD, history of treatment-emergent mania/hypomania on antidepressant, bipolar temperament.

C4 Major Depressive Disorder
▾

Mnemonic: SIG E CAPS

Sleep, Interest, Guilt, Energy, Concentration, Appetite, Psychomotor, Suicidal. Criterion A requires ≥5 of 9 symptoms for ≥2 weeks; one must be depressed mood or anhedonia.

C5 Substance Use Disorder — Alcohol & Other Substances
▾

ICD-11 four-tier hierarchy vs ICD-10 binary vs DSM-5-TR unified spectrum

ICD-11: Episode of harmful use → Harmful pattern → Hazardous use (chapter 24) → Substance dependence. DSM-5-TR: Mild (2–3 criteria), Moderate (4–5), Severe (≥6) out of 11 criteria. Document temporal pattern, quantity/frequency, withdrawal, tolerance.

C6 Anxiety & Panic Disorder
▾

Mandatory organic screen

TSH is mandatory for all anxiety presentations. Differentials include hyperthyroidism, phaeochromocytoma (rare), cardiac arrhythmia, asthma, alcohol/benzodiazepine withdrawal, stimulant intoxication.

C7 OCD & Obsessive-Compulsive Related Disorders
▾

Key discriminator: ego-dystonic + neutralising compulsions

OCD-defining features: obsessions are intrusive, unwanted, distressing; compulsions are repetitive behaviours or mental acts performed in response; symptoms take up a substantial part of the day. BABS scale grades insight.

  • vs schizophrenia: ego-dystonic quality, no other psychotic phenomena, BABS helps.
  • vs OCPD: ego-dystonic vs ego-syntonic perfectionism.
  • vs ASD: rituals are anxiety-neutralisation driven, not sensory-/sameness-driven.
C8 PTSD & Complex PTSD
▾

CPTSD vs BPD — now an examinable differential

ICD-11 recognises CPTSD (6B41) separately from PTSD (6B40). Requires 3 PTSD clusters PLUS 3 DSO. DSM-5-TR does not recognise CPTSD. Discriminator from BPD: trauma history (prolonged interpersonal trauma required), persistent negative self-concept vs unstable identity oscillation.

C9 Eating Disorder — Anorexia Nervosa & Related
▾

Mandatory BMI and organic screen

AN criterion: significantly low body weight (BMI <18.5 adults; <5th centile children). Always check BMI. Differentials: depression with weight loss (no fear of weight gain), hyperthyroidism (TSH, free T4), malignancy (CBC, CRP, ESR), ARFID (no body image concern), psychotic disorder with food refusal.

C10 Dissociative / Functional Neurological Symptom Disorder
▾

Positive clinical signs of FND matter

Hoover's sign (functional weakness), tremor entrainment test, dissociative seizure semiology. La belle indifference is non-specific and overrated. The workup must rule out organic neurological disease before diagnosing FND.

C11 Personality Disorder (ICD-11 Dimensional Model)
▾

The state-versus-trait trap — highest-yield viva failure

Do NOT diagnose PD during an acute Axis I episode. A manic patient looks histrionic/grandiose; a depressed patient looks dependent/avoidant; a psychotic patient looks schizotypal. Defer PD diagnosis until Axis I episode has remitted. Document this rationale explicitly.

C12 Delirium & Neurocognitive Disorder / Dementia
▾

The single most important question: "Was this person sharp last month?"

Delirium = acute (hours–days), fluctuating, attention impaired, consciousness altered. Dementia = insidious, progressive, consciousness preserved, multiple cognitive domains affected. If acute onset + fluctuating → delirium until proved otherwise.

D Six Worked Differentials — Viva Traps
▾

Template for every differential answer

State working diagnosis → name 2–3 alternatives in ranked order → give discriminating features → conclude with single most decisive feature. Do not list >3 differentials (signals confusion). Do list at least 2 (signals discipline).

D1. Psychotic Mania vs Schizoaffective vs Schizophrenia with Mood Symptoms

Stem: 28-year-old man, 1 month grandiose delusions, reduced sleep, pressured speech, auditory hallucinations of God, irritability. 3 years ago similar episode responded to lithium + olanzapine. Between episodes, fully himself, working as lawyer.

D2. Agitated Depression vs Mixed Mania vs Anxious Depression

Stem: 38-year-old woman, 2-month low mood, anhedonia, early morning wakening, weight loss, profound fatigue, but also restlessness, racing thoughts that feel out of control, irritability, brief periods of feeling "wired but exhausted". No prior mania. Family history of BD in maternal aunt.

D3. OCD vs Schizophrenia with Delusion-like Obsessions vs ASD with Rituals

Stem: 22-year-old man, 4-year contamination concerns, hand-washing 30–40 times/day, 3-hour showers, refusal to touch door handles. Insight fluctuating: some days says "I know it's silly but I can't stop", other days insists contamination is real.

D4. Anorexia Nervosa vs MDD with Weight Loss vs Hyperthyroidism vs Malignancy

Stem: 19-year-old woman, 12-month progressive weight loss 52kg → 38kg, BMI 14.2, restricted intake ~600kcal/day, compulsive exercise 2 hours daily, amenorrhoea 9 months, layered clothing in warm weather, perceives herself as still fat.

D5. FND vs Feigned/Factitious vs Neurological Disease

Stem: 24-year-old woman, sudden right leg weakness 3 days after marital argument. Hoover's sign positive, normal tone and reflexes, no UMN signs. Describes weakness in dramatic terms with mildly elevated affect (la belle indifference).

D6. Delirium vs Dementia vs Depressive Pseudodementia vs Late-Onset Psychosis vs Late-Onset Mania

Stem: 82-year-old man, 48 hours confusion. Previously sharp, living independently. New medication for back pain last month. Disoriented to time and place, fluctuating attention, visual hallucinations of small animals, agitated by evening, calmer by morning.

G Pitfalls & Exam Traps — Self-Audit Checklist
▾

Systematic self-check before finalising diagnosis

Use this checklist to audit your diagnostic documentation. Each unchecked item is a potential viva trap.

Trap 1: Listing one diagnosis without ranked differential. Have you named a primary working diagnosis plus 2–3 ranked alternatives with discriminators?
Trap 2: Missing Axis III (organic cause). Have you forced the organic question — TSH, B12, structural causes, autoimmune encephalitis?
Trap 3: Missing Axis IV. Have you documented psychosocial context? Even "no significant stressor identified" is an acceptable line.
Trap 4: Diagnosing personality disorder during acute Axis I episode. State-vs-trait trap. Have you deferred PD assessment or justified why state symptoms have settled?
Trap 5: Diagnosing schizophrenia without respecting duration thresholds. Brief psychotic disorder <1 month; schizophreniform 1–6 months (DSM); schizophrenia ≥1 month (ICD) or ≥6 months (DSM).
Trap 6: Conflating ICD-10 and ICD-11 codes. F31.2 is ICD-10; 6A60.4 is ICD-11. They are not interchangeable. Pick one system per line.
Trap 7: Confusing diagnosis with formulation. Diagnosis names; formulation explains. Both are required in a complete write-up.
Trap 8: Over-using "NOS" or "unspecified". Reserve for genuinely atypical presentations, not as a way of avoiding diagnostic commitment.
Trap 9: Forgetting cultural framing. A patient who describes "pet mein gola" or "nazar lagna" is giving you their explanatory model. Engage with it.
Trap 10: Treating burnout as a psychiatric diagnosis. ICD-11 codes burnout (QD85) as occupational phenomenon, chapter 24. If criteria for adjustment disorder, depression, or anxiety are met, diagnose those.
Trap 11: Using abolished schizophrenia subtypes in ICD-11. Paranoid, hebephrenic, catatonic schizophrenia are ICD-10 categories, gone in ICD-11. Use symptomatic specifiers instead.
Trap 12: Diagnosing AN without checking BMI. BMI <18.5 in adults is the criterion. A patient with cognitive features of AN at normal BMI may meet criteria for atypical anorexia or bulimia nervosa.
Trap 13: Missing Complex PTSD. In the Indian context (childhood adversity, intergenerational trauma, gender-based violence) CPTSD is often more accurate than PTSD alone.
H Sample Viva Stems — Practice Delivery
▾

Practice these stems until delivery is automatic

These are not scripts to memorise word-for-word, but structural templates to populate with your patient's data. Deliver with diagnostic humility and commitment.

Generic 4-Axis Stem

"My diagnostic formulation in this patient is as follows. Axis I: [primary diagnosis with severity, course, current state, ICD-11 code, ICD-10 code if needed], with [comorbid diagnosis] as a comorbid Axis I diagnosis. Axis II: [personality disorder grading or 'no personality disorder identified, deferred until acute episode resolves'] and [intellectual disability if relevant]. Axis III: [medical conditions]. Axis IV: [psychosocial stressors with severity rating], with current functioning estimated at [GAF or WHODAS]."

Diagnosis with Ranked Differential Stem

"The working diagnosis is [primary], with [differential 1] and [differential 2] on the differential, ranked in that order. The discriminating features are [feature A favouring primary over D1], [feature B favouring primary over D2], and the single most decisive feature in this patient is [feature]. I would re-evaluate the diagnosis at [follow-up point] when [pending information] becomes available."

Formulation Paragraph Stem

"Predisposing factors include [biological loading], [psychological vulnerability, schemas, attachment, temperament], and [social factors, family structure, childhood adversity]. Precipitating factors are [recent medical, psychological, social trigger]. Perpetuating factors include [ongoing biological, psychological, social factors]. Protective factors are [strengths in each domain]. The formulation suggests that the highest-yield interventions in the immediate to short-term phase are [targeted intervention 1] and [targeted intervention 2]."

Cultural Formulation Stem

"On cultural formulation, the patient describes the problem as [patient's own framing, translate idiom of distress]. The cultural attribution of cause is [evil eye / possession / karma / family curse / scientific explanation / mixed]. Prior help-seeking has included [traditional healer / faith healer / AYUSH / allopathic / self-medication / family advice]. Concerns about the current consultation include [stigma / cost / family attendance / language / gender of clinician]. The cultural framework will inform the engagement strategy by [specific intervention]."

ICD-11 vs ICD-10 Comparison Stem

"The primary diagnosis in ICD-11 is [code, name with specifiers]. In ICD-10 the corresponding code would be [F-code, name]. The substantive difference between the two is [the relevant ICD-11 reform]. In DSM-5-TR the equivalent diagnosis is [code, name], differing from ICD-11 in [duration requirement, specifier, threshold]."

Personality Disorder ICD-11 Stem

"The patient meets criteria for personality disorder under ICD-11. Severity is [mild / moderate / severe]. The trait domain qualifiers are [negative affectivity / detachment / dissociality / disinhibition / anankastia], with [most prominent] most prominent. The borderline pattern qualifier [is / is not] applicable, given [evidence of BPD pattern]. The ICD-10 categorical equivalent would have been [F60.x]. The diagnosis was made after the acute Axis I episode had partially resolved to avoid the state-versus-trait trap."

CPTSD vs BPD Stem

"In this patient with chronic affective dysregulation, negative self-concept, interpersonal difficulties, and re-experiencing of childhood abuse, the differential is between Complex PTSD (ICD-11: 6B41) and Borderline Personality Disorder. The discriminating features are: (1) [documented prolonged interpersonal trauma], which is a core feature of CPTSD and not required for BPD; (2) [persistent negative self-concept], which is more characteristic of CPTSD than the unstable identity of BPD; (3) [re-experiencing cluster], which is required for CPTSD and not part of BPD criteria. I favour [CPTSD / BPD] as the primary diagnosis. DSM-5-TR does not recognise CPTSD; in DSM-5-TR I would code this as [PTSD with dissociative features / comorbid BPD]."

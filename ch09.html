Catatonia Bedside Screen (2 minutes)

  • Waxy flexibility — limb retains position against gravity; "bending a wax candle" quality
  • Posturing — spontaneous unusual postures; pillow sign (head held above bed)
  • Gegenhalten — resistance proportional to examiner force
  • Mitgehen — anglepoise lamp sign; limb moves with lightest touch
  • Grasp reflex — stroking palm elicits unreleasable grasp
  • Echopraxia / echolalia — mirroring examiner
  • Automatic obedience — follows absurd instructions
  • Negativism — does opposite of request

Positive screen → Bush-Francis Catatonia Rating Scale + urgent lorazepam trial

EPS Bedside Screen (<1 minute)

  • Parkinsonism — masked facies, reduced blink, resting tremor, cogwheel rigidity, bradykinesia, shuffling gait
  • Acute dystonia — torticollis, trismus, oculogyric crisis; emergency; anticholinergic antidote
  • Akathisia — inner restlessness + observable inability to sit still; often missed
  • Tardive dyskinesia — choreiform face/tongue/trunk movements; late, long-term antipsychotic use

Why This Matters

Four operational items must be in place before the examination begins. The GPE is mandatory in Mental Healthcare Act 2017 admission paperwork. Missing consent or chaperone documentation is a medico-legal failure.

A1. Consent, Chaperone, Positioning, Privacy
Sample Phrasings

G1. Consent opening: "I'd like to examine you physically now, which I do for every patient. It will take about ten minutes. I'll explain what I'm doing at each step. You can stop me at any time if anything feels uncomfortable. Is that alright?"

G2. Chaperone invitation: "I'll ask the staff nurse to stay with us during the examination, that's the usual practice when I examine any female patient. Is that alright?"

G3. Position and preparation: "I'll ask you to lie down on the couch. I'll cover you with the sheet, and I'll only uncover the part I'm examining at each step. If you feel cold or uncomfortable, please let me know."

G4. Explaining each step: "I'm going to take your blood pressure now, you'll feel the cuff tighten for a few seconds." / "I'm going to listen to your heart, I'll need to put the stethoscope on your chest." / "I'm going to press gently on your tummy to check there's nothing unusual, tell me if anything hurts."

G7. Closing: "That's everything I needed to check. You can sit up now. Thank you, I'll cover what I found when we talk about the next steps." Offer privacy to dress before continuing conversation.

Documentation Convention

Explicit verbal consent documented. Chaperone named in file (staff nurse > female colleague > family member). Written consent for inpatient admissions.

Why This Matters

The general appearance is documented first and in summary form. Apparent age compared to stated age is a key clue: older than stated age suggests chronic alcohol use, severe depression, advanced eating disorder, or chronic illness. Do not estimate BMI — calculate from measured weight and height.

A2. General Appearance, Build, and Nutritional Status
Documentation Convention

Document factual, non-judgmental descriptions. The register here is physical (contrasts with behavioural register in MSE Chapter 11).

Build Types
  • Endomorphic — round, soft, fat-predominant
  • Mesomorphic — muscular, athletic
  • Ectomorphic — lean, thin, linear
  • Cachectic — severely wasted, indicating chronic illness or malnutrition
Nutritional Status

Well-nourished / Under-nourished / Over-nourished. BMI is the objective measure.

e.g., Patient is middle-aged female, lean build, normal BMI. Appears older than stated age.

Why This Matters

Each vital sign carries a psychiatric interpretive layer. Temperature abnormalities raise suspicion for NMS, serotonin syndrome, withdrawal, or toxicity. Pulse <50 in anorexia is a medical emergency. Orthostatic BP must be taken in every patient on antipsychotics or TCAs (drop ≥20 systolic or ≥10 diastolic = falls risk).

A3. Vital Signs
Psychiatric Interpretation Quick Guide
  • Hyperthermia: NMS, serotonin syndrome, alcohol/benzo withdrawal, sympathomimetic intoxication, infection (delirium), lithium toxicity
  • Hypothermia: Severe anorexia nervosa, hypothyroidism, exposure, sepsis in frail
  • Tachycardia: Anxiety, withdrawal, stimulant intoxication, anticholinergic toxicity, hyperthyroidism, serotonin syndrome, NMS, dehydration, anaemia, heart failure, psychotropics
  • Bradycardia (<50 in anorexia = admit): Anorexia nervosa, beta-blockers, cholinergic overdose, athletic variant, hypothyroidism
  • Orthostatic drop: Antipsychotics (alpha-1 blockade — worse with clozapine, quetiapine, low-potency FGAs), TCAs, dehydration, autonomic neuropathy, adrenal insufficiency
  • Hypertension: Stimulant intoxication, MAOI-tyramine crisis, cannabinoid hyperemesis, untreated primary HTN, pheochromocytoma
  • Widened pulse pressure: Hyperthyroidism, aortic regurgitation, high output states
  • Tachypnoea: Acute anxiety/panic, salicylate overdose, pneumonia (institutionalised schizophrenia, alcohol), PE in immobile
  • Respiratory depression: Opioid overdose, benzo overdose, severe CNS depressants

Why This Matters

The skin carries a substantial load of psychiatric-specific findings. Self-harm scars, injection marks, and drug-related rashes are clinical data, not incidental observations. Documentation must be factual and non-judgmental. Any rash in a patient on lamotrigine is Stevens-Johnson syndrome until proven otherwise.

A5. Skin Examination
Self-Harm Scar Documentation Convention

Document: location, number, pattern, apparent chronology (fresh/healing/old), laterality.

Factual, non-judgmental: "multiple linear scars of varying age on the left forearm, healed" — NOT "typical self-harm scars."

Right-handed patients typically have left forearm scars.

Injection Mark Location Discrimination
  • Antecubital / forearm: IV substance use
  • Buttock / deltoid: Prescribed depot antipsychotic
  • Groin / femoral: Long-term IV drug user (peripheral sites exhausted)
  • Between toes: Concealment site, high-yield

  • Valproate: Alopecia, generalized maculopapular rash (rare)

Why This Matters

The systemic examination is delivered in summary form in the psychiatric GPE. The four systems (CVS, Respiratory, Per abdomen, CNS) are covered at a level adequate to detect findings that matter psychiatrically. The detailed neurological examination lives in Chapter 10.

A6. Systemic Examination (Summary Level)
CVS Psychiatric Relevance

Murmurs (infective endocarditis in IVDU; MVP in panic disorder; rheumatic valve disease in older Indian patients). S3/S4 (heart failure — lithium relevance). Arrhythmias (QTc-prolonging psychotropics; alcohol withdrawal).

Respiratory Psychiatric Relevance

Pneumonia in institutionalised schizophrenia and alcohol cases. Wheeze if beta-blockers being considered. Basal crackles of CHF.

Abdominal Psychiatric Relevance

Hepatomegaly = highest-yield finding in alcohol use case. Splenomegaly, ascites, caput medusae in decompensated cirrhosis. Parotid enlargement in chronic alcohol use and bulimia nervosa.

CNS Summary Level

Gross motor and sensory screen, gait if safe, special signs in A7. Detailed cranial nerves, motor/sensory, coordination, reflexes in Chapter 10.

Why This Matters

Three acute safety bedside screens live at GPE level because a positive finding changes management immediately. Catatonia is under-recognised; the screen takes 2 minutes. EPS screen takes <1 minute. NMS and serotonin syndrome are medical emergencies with specific bedside discriminations.

A7. Special Signs — Acute Safety Bedside Screens
NMS vs Serotonin Syndrome Discrimination

NMS rigidity = lead-pipe with reduced reflexes. Serotonin syndrome rigidity = hyperreflexic with clonus. Clonus is the single most specific bedside sign for serotonin syndrome.

History of serotonergic agent exposure (SSRI, SNRI, MAOI, tramadol, linezolid, triptans) is the starting point for serotonin syndrome.

Why This Matters

The framing is delivered verbally before the examination begins. The critical element is the explicit statement that the examination is routine and offered to every patient, which lowers perceived exceptionality and resistance. For inpatient admissions, consent is documented in writing.

C1. Consent and Framing

Why This Matters

The examination sequence maps roughly to head-to-toe and minimises the number of times the patient changes position. Following a standard sequence ensures nothing is missed and the documentation order is consistent.

C2. Examination Sequence
Standard Sequence
  1. Vital signs (patient seated)
  2. General appearance (patient seated, observed during consent)
  3. Hands (seated)
  4. Head and neck, scleral icterus, oral mucosa, lymph nodes, thyroid (seated)
  5. Patient moves to couch, lies supine, draped
  6. Cardiovascular (supine, head at 45°)
  7. Respiratory (supine, then sitting forward for posterior chest)
  8. Abdominal (supine)
  9. Lower limbs and peripheral pulses (supine)
  10. Special signs (catatonia, EPS, NMS/SS) as indicated

Why This Matters

The psychiatric GPE is documented as a series of one-line entries per system. Each line is either negative in structured form (PICCLE documented as explicit absence) or positive with specific description. The catatonia and EPS screens are documented only when the clinical picture warranted the screen.

Why This Matters

English only for this chapter. Physical examination consent scripts are procedural and the content does not benefit from the trilingual register that serves history-taking chapters. Having standard scripts reduces patient anxiety and ensures consistent consent.

G1. Consent Opening

"I'd like to examine you physically now, which I do for every patient. It will take about ten minutes. I'll explain what I'm doing at each step. You can stop me at any time if anything feels uncomfortable. Is that alright?"

G2. Chaperone Invitation

"I'll ask the staff nurse to stay with us during the examination, that's the usual practice when I examine any female patient. Is that alright?"

G3. Position and Preparation

"I'll ask you to lie down on the couch. I'll cover you with the sheet, and I'll only uncover the part I'm examining at each step. If you feel cold or uncomfortable, please let me know."

G4. Explaining Each Step

BP: "I'm going to take your blood pressure now, you'll feel the cuff tighten for a few seconds."
Heart: "I'm going to listen to your heart, I'll need to put the stethoscope on your chest."
Abdomen: "I'm going to press gently on your tummy to check there's nothing unusual, tell me if anything hurts."
Movement: "I'm going to check the movement in your arms and legs, I'll move them for you, you don't need to do anything."

G5. Catatonia Bedside Screen (Examiner-Directed)

"I'm going to gently move your arm, let me do the work, you can relax." Observe for waxy flexibility as limb is moved and after release. Observe for Gegenhalten during movement. Test mitgehen by lifting limb with minimal contact. Stroke palm for grasp reflex. Observe for posturing throughout. Ask absurd question for automatic obedience. Give opposing instruction for negativism.

G6. EPS Bedside Screen (Examiner-Directed)

"I'm going to move your wrist, tell me if it's uncomfortable." Passive flexion/extension of wrist observing cogwheel rigidity. Observe face for masked facies and reduced blinking. Ask patient to stand and walk, observing shuffling gait, reduced arm swing, postural instability. Ask about subjective inner restlessness and observe for objective fidgeting.

G7. Closing the Examination

"That's everything I needed to check. You can sit up now. Thank you, I'll cover what I found when we talk about the next steps." Offer privacy to dress and rearrange before conversation continues.

A NIMHANS Framework

▼

Why This Framework Matters

  • Investigations serve 4 ordered functions: rule-out organic → establish baseline → monitor safety → confirm rare differentials.
  • In viva, "routine bloods" is not an answer. Every test needs a stated justification tied to this patient.
  • Missing an organic cause is the highest-cost single error in clinical practice and examinations.

A1 Rule Out Organic Mimics

▼
Tick all that apply to this patient. These are the red-flag presentations from the textbook that mandate screening for organic causes. Missing an organic mimic is the highest-cost single error.
List the specific mimics you are actively screening for based on this patient's presentation. High-yield mimics: autoimmune encephalitis (anti-NMDA, anti-VGKC), hypothyroidism/hyperthyroidism, B12 deficiency (common in Indian vegetarians), hypercortisolism (Cushing), Wilson disease (<40 + movement disorder), neurosyphilis, HIV-CNS, electrolyte derangement, substance intoxication/withdrawal.

A2 Establish Baseline Before Psychotropic

▶
Anything you will measure as a side effect later must be measured before the drug starts. Document baseline to prove change is drug-related. Lithium needs renal, thyroid, cardiac. Valproate needs LFT, FBC, MHRA pregnancy declaration. Clozapine needs CBC, ECG, ECHO/troponin. Olanzapine needs metabolic baseline.
Tick every baseline test you will order before starting medication. The principle is: anything you will measure as a side effect later must be measured before the drug starts.
Document any additional baseline tests with explicit justification. e.g., Serum calcium (lithium hyperparathyroidism risk), Troponin + ECHO (clozapine myocarditis baseline), HLA-B*15:02 (before carbamazepine in Indian patients).

A3 Monitor Drug Safety & Response

▶
Tick all monitoring domains applicable to this patient's medication plan. Drug monitoring is not optional — every drug named in Chapter 14 carries a monitoring plan specified in Section D of this chapter.

A4 Confirm Specific Differentials (Rare)

▶
These are the moments when the lab actually makes the diagnosis. They are uncommon but career-defining. List confirmatory tests with the specific differential they evaluate. Examples: serum copper + ceruloplasmin + 24h urinary copper (Wilson disease), anti-NMDA receptor antibodies (autoimmune encephalitis), MRI mesial temporal sclerosis (temporal lobe epilepsy), anti-TPO + cortisol screen + anti-dsDNA (specific endocrine/autoimmune).

B Why It Matters — Judging the Investigation Plan

▶

Exam Pearl

When asked "what investigations would you order?" never list every test you can think of. The graded answer is risk-stratified: a baseline panel that you would order in any patient, plus a small number of targeted tests justified by features of this patient. The candidate who lists ten tests with reasons beats the candidate who lists thirty without.

B1 Targeting

▶
Self-audit: can you defend every test against a specific organic possibility, drug-monitoring requirement, or severity-tracking question? "Routine bloods" is never a defensible answer in viva.
Write the viva-ready justification for each test. Model format: "CBC for clozapine baseline; TSH to rule out hypothyroidism mimicking depression; fasting glucose because patient is starting olanzapine and has family history of diabetes."

B2 Sequencing

▶
Group tests by result turnaround to show examiner you understand clinical decision urgency. 30 minutes: CBC, U&E, glucose, urinalysis, ABG, ECG, point-of-care toxicology. 24 hours: LFT, TFT, lipids, B12, vitamin D. Days: autoimmune panels, MRI scheduling, HLA-B*15:02, anti-NMDA.
The order in which results return determines the order in which decisions can be made. Show the examiner your decision tree: immediate results guide acute medical decisions; 24-hour results guide organic mimic rule-out; days-long results guide differential confirmation.

B3 Indian-Context Realism

▶
The examiner tests realism. A district hospital in Karnataka does not have anti-NMDA-receptor antibody testing on tap. Know cost (~₹6000–8000, 1–2 weeks via NIMHANS or private lab) and turnaround before ordering high-cost tests.
Document awareness of test cost and availability. Reference: TSH ~₹150 (24 hr), MRI brain ₹3000–8000 (1–3 days), anti-NMDA antibody ₹6000–8000 (1–2 weeks send-out), HLA-B*15:02 ₹2500–4000 (3–7 days), clozapine level ₹1500–3000.

B4 Drug Monitoring is Not Optional

▶
A management plan that does not specify monitoring is incomplete. An investigation plan that does not match the management plan is incoherent. The two chapters are paired and should be defended as a unit in viva.

Clinical Anchor

In KMCRI viva, examiners listen for two things: (1) the candidate names the first-line panel without prompting, (2) the candidate adds one or two targeted tests with a stated justification. A candidate who orders a targeted test "because of the family history of autoimmune disease in the patient's mother" is doing it right.

C First-Episode Workup by Clinical Scenario

▶

How to Use This Section

  • Each block is a worked answer for the corresponding presentation (maps to Chapters 03, 11, 14).
  • First six items in each panel are the baseline; targeted additions follow with their rationale.
  • IPS-CPG 2026 is the primary citation for first-episode workup in the four major disorders.

C1 First-Episode Psychosis (Any Age)

▶
CBC with differential, fasting glucose, HbA1c, fasting lipids, LFT, RFT, electrolytes (Na, K, Ca, Mg, PO4), TSH (mandatory — hypothyroidism mimics negative-symptom psychosis), vitamin B12 & folate (B12 deficiency in vegetarians is common in India), vitamin D, urinalysis, urine pregnancy test in women of reproductive age, urine toxicology screen (cannabis, opioids, amphetamines, BZDs, cocaine), ECG (baseline before any QT-prolonging antipsychotic), HIV serology and VDRL/RPR.
MRI brain: first episode at any age (IPS-CPG 2026 Level 2), focal signs, atypical features (rapid onset, non-auditory hallucinations, age <14 or >40, seizures, dyskinesias, cognitive decline, poor treatment response). CT only if MRI unavailable. EEG: seizure phenomenology (deja vu, jamais vu), catatonia, non-convulsive status epilepticus. Autoimmune encephalitis screen: anti-NMDA-R, anti-VGKC (LGI1, CASPR2), anti-GABA-B, anti-AMPA, anti-GAD65, paraneoplastic panel — red flags: subacute onset <3mo, dyskinesias, seizures, autonomic instability, cognitive decline, decreased consciousness, autoimmune FH, viral prodrome, ovarian teratoma, refractoriness to antipsychotics. ANA/C3/C4/anti-dsDNA for suspected SLE. Copper/ceruloplasmin/24h urinary copper + slit-lamp KF rings for Wilson's (<40 + movement disorder). HLA-B*15:02 before carbamazepine in South/East Asian. Pre-clozapine: CBC (ANC ≥3.0), troponin + ECHO baseline, LFT, glucose, lipids, weight.

C2 First Episode of Mania (Bipolar I)

▶
CBC, RFT (urea, creatinine, eGFR), LFT (AST, ALT, ALP, bilirubin, albumin, total protein), TSH (mandatory — hyperthyroidism mimics mania), fasting glucose, HbA1c, fasting lipids, electrolytes including calcium (lithium-associated hyperparathyroidism is a long-term risk), urine pregnancy test in women of reproductive age (pregnancy reshapes the entire mood-stabiliser choice), urinalysis, urine toxicology, ECG (baseline before lithium and antipsychotics, QTc).
MRI brain: first manic episode aged ≥40 (late-onset mania has much higher rate of organic causes — vascular, frontotemporal, post-stroke, tumour); also for any focal signs or atypical features at any age. EEG: if seizures suspected, post-ictal mania, complex partial seizure phenomenology. HLA-B*15:02 before carbamazepine in Indian patients. Anti-thyroid antibodies (anti-TPO): if subclinical hypothyroidism or first-episode mania at unusual age (Hashimoto encephalopathy, postpartum thyroiditis). Cortisol screen (overnight dexamethasone suppression or 24h urinary free cortisol): Cushing syndrome can present with mania.
YMRS (Young Mania Rating Scale, 11 items) — clinician-rated gold standard. 0–12 remission, 13–19 minimal symptoms, 20–25 mild, 26–37 moderate, ≥38 severe.
0–12 remission | 13–19 minimal | 20–25 mild | 26–37 moderate | ≥38 severe

C3 First Episode of Major Depression

▶
CBC, RFT, LFT, TSH (mandatory — hypothyroid depression is a high-yield missed diagnosis), B12, folate, vitamin D, fasting glucose, HbA1c, fasting lipids, electrolytes, urinalysis, urine pregnancy test, ECG (≥40 years or cardiac history / before any QT-prolonging antidepressant or antipsychotic).
HIV serology: in any patient with risk factors; HIV-related depression is treatable and underdiagnosed. Cortisol screen: Cushingoid features. Calcium/PTH: hypercalcaemia of any cause can present with depression. 24h urinary catecholamines or plasma metanephrines: phaeochromocytoma (rare but classical mimic of agitated depression with anxiety). Sleep study: chronic refractory depression with prominent sleep disturbance; OSA is a treatable cause. Neuroimaging: first episode after age 50, focal neurological signs, treatment-resistant depression, prominent cognitive features (vascular depression, frontal-subcortical pattern, NPH, FTD presenting as depression). Vitamin D: independently associated with mood symptoms in Indian populations; supplement if deficient.
PHQ-9: 0–4 minimal, 5–9 mild, 10–14 moderate, 15–19 moderately severe, ≥20 severe. Item 9 = suicide screen. IPS-CPG 2026 recommends ≥1 validated severity measure tracked across visits. PHQ-9 ≥25% reduction at week 4 = continue; <25% = increase or switch. HAM-D: 0–7 normal, 8–13 mild, 14–18 moderate, 19–22 severe, ≥23 very severe.

C4 OCD (First Presentation)

▶
OCD baseline workup is minimal in the absence of red flags: CBC, TSH, RFT, LFT, fasting glucose and lipids (because high-dose SSRI treatment is long-term and the patient may need cardiometabolic baseline if augmentation with an antipsychotic becomes necessary).
ASO titre, anti-DNase B, throat swab, and broader anti-basal-ganglia antibody panel: pediatric or adolescent acute-onset OCD with neuropsychiatric features (PANDAS / PANS workup; rare in adults, brief mention). Neuroimaging: atypical onset (very early or very late), focal neurological signs, hard-to-treat OCD with motor features (Sydenham chorea, Tourette overlap). Pregnancy test: before SSRI initiation in women of reproductive age.
Y-BOCS (Yale-Brown Obsessive Compulsive Scale): 10 items, 5 obsessions + 5 compulsions. 0–7 subclinical, 8–15 mild, 16–23 moderate, 24–31 severe, 32–40 extreme. Administer at presentation, monthly, and at end of treatment. BABS (Brown Assessment of Beliefs Scale): insight quantification in OCD and delusion-spectrum beliefs.

C5 Substance Use Disorder — Alcohol

▶
CBC (macrocytosis MCV>100fL — normal B12 and folate makes this alcohol-specific), GGT (elevated in chronic use, falls with abstinence), AST/ALT (AST:ALT >2 suggests alcoholic hepatitis), bilirubin, albumin, INR/PT (synthetic liver function), urea (often low in malnourished alcohol users), creatinine, electrolytes (Na, K, Mg, PO4, Ca — all commonly deranged in withdrawal), glucose, HbA1c, lipids, amylase & lipase if pancreatitis, ammonia if hepatic encephalopathy, urine/breath alcohol, urine toxicology, ECG, HIV, HBsAg, anti-HCV. Thiamine empirical: do NOT wait for result; give before any glucose load.
Abdominal US/CT: suspected cirrhosis, ascites, hepatocellular carcinoma in long-term users. Upper GI endoscopy: varices, peptic ulcer disease. Cardiac echo: alcoholic cardiomyopathy. MRI brain: Wernicke encephalopathy (mammillary body atrophy, periaqueductal grey hyperintensity), hepatic encephalopathy (basal ganglia T1 hyperintensity from manganese deposition), Marchiafava-Bignami disease (corpus callosum atrophy), chronic alcohol-related dementia.
CIWA-Ar (Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment for Alcohol, revised, 10 items) — bedside withdrawal severity instrument; drives symptom-triggered benzodiazepine dosing. Hourly until <10, then 4-hourly.

C6 Anxiety / Panic Disorder

▶
TSH (mandatory — hyperthyroidism mimics generalised anxiety and panic), ECG (cardiac mimic and baseline before SSRI/SNRI), CBC, RFT, LFT, fasting glucose, electrolytes including calcium and magnesium (hypocalcaemia and hypomagnesaemia present with paraesthesiae and anxiety).
24h catecholamines / plasma metanephrines: phaeochromocytoma if labile blood pressure or paroxysmal hypertension accompanies panic attacks. Cardiac evaluation (ECG, echo, Holter): if cardiac differential is on the table — the typical patient who has been worked up by a cardiologist before reaching psychiatry. Pulmonary function tests / chest X-ray: if breathlessness component is unexplained (rule out obstructive lung disease, pulmonary embolism in acute first attack).
HAM-A (14-item, clinician-rated): <17 mild, 18–24 mild-to-moderate, 25–30 moderate-to-severe. GAD-7 (7-item, self-report): 0–4 minimal, 5–9 mild, 10–14 moderate, ≥15 severe. PDSS: panic disorder severity scale — clinician-rated. Track at presentation and follow-up.

C7 Eating Disorder — Anorexia Nervosa

▶
The AN baseline panel is the most extensive workup in adult psychiatry because mortality is real and acute. Red flags: postural BP drop >20 mmHg systolic or symptomatic = red flag; bradycardia <40 = red flag; hypothermia <35.5C = red flag; hypoglycaemia = red flag; QTc >450 ms = red flag; weight loss >1 kg/week = red flag.
CBC (anaemia, leucopenia, thrombocytopenia all common). Full electrolyte panel including potassium, magnesium, phosphate — the refeeding panel. Hypophosphataemia within 72 hours of refeeding is the cardinal feature of refeeding syndrome and is fatal. Sodium, calcium, urea, creatinine, glucose. LFT: transaminitis is common in severe AN, often resolves with refeeding. Albumin: typically preserved until very late — a low albumin in AN is sinister. TFT: low T3 syndrome (euthyroid sick) is universal and does NOT require treatment. Cortisol, LH/FSH/oestradiol (functional hypothalamic amenorrhoea), CK (rhabdomyolysis from compulsive exercise), zinc.

C8 Delirium — Cause-Finding Panel

▶
The delirium workup is the cause-finding panel. Goal: identify and treat the precipitating cause as fast as possible. CBC, U&E, calcium, magnesium, phosphate, glucose, LFT, TFT, B12, folate, vitamin D, CRP, blood cultures (if febrile), urinalysis & urine culture, chest X-ray, ECG, ABG (especially in elderly — look for hypoxia, hypercapnia, acidosis), pulse oximetry continuous, drug levels for any drug with measurable level (digoxin, lithium, anticonvulsants, theophylline), urine toxicology, ammonia (if hepatic disease), COVID-19 swab, influenza swab.
CT head: focal neurological signs, anticoagulated patient with possible intracranial bleed, fall with head injury, unexplained delirium without obvious systemic cause. Lumbar puncture: meningitis, encephalitis, subarachnoid haemorrhage suspected after CT clear. EEG: non-convulsive status epilepticus is a treatable and underrecognised cause of delirium in the elderly. MRI brain: when CT is non-diagnostic and structural cause remains suspected.

C9 Dementia (IPS-CPG 2018, Shaji KS et al)

▶
MMSE/HMSE: global screen; MMSE cutoff <24 (education-adjusted). HMSE = validated Indian version for Hindi-speaking patients. MoCA: more sensitive to MCI and executive deficits; cutoff <26 (subtract 1 if ≤12 years education). Available in Hindi, Tamil, Telugu, Marathi, Bengali, Kannada. ACE-III: more sensitive to early FTD and posterior cortical presentations. GDS: depression mimics dementia (pseudodementia).
MRI brain preferred over CT. At minimum to assess hippocampal volume (Alzheimer's pattern), cortical atrophy distribution (frontotemporal, posterior cortical), white matter hyperintensities (vascular contribution), normal-pressure hydrocephalus, chronic subdural haematoma, tumour. CT acceptable where MRI unavailable.

C10 Personality / Dissociative / FND / PTSD

▶
Workup is minimal for these presentations — diagnosis is clinical, not laboratory-based. Over-investigation risks iatrogenic harm and reinforces medical-model framing. Document the decision explicitly: "no further investigations indicated; the workup is clinical." Iatrogenic harm from over-investigation is a recognised pitfall in FND particularly.
Document explicitly in the case sheet. This demonstrates to the examiner that you have thought about investigations and decided not to over-investigate. The refusal stem is a scored element in viva.

D1 Mood Stabilisers

▶
Baseline: RFT (urea, creatinine, eGFR), TSH, calcium, ECG (≥40 or cardiac history), pregnancy test, weight, BMI, urinalysis. Ongoing: Serum lithium 12h post-dose on day 5; then every 3 months × 2; then 6-monthly. eGFR and TSH every 6 months. Calcium annually. Weight at every visit. TDM: 0.6–1.2 mEq/L acute mania; 0.4–0.8 maintenance/elderly. Toxicity ≥1.5; severe ≥2.0.
Baseline: LFT, FBC, weight, MHRA pregnancy declaration (mandatory for women of reproductive age, MHRA 2024 restriction in <55 years), BMI. Ongoing: LFT and FBC at 3 months then 6-monthly. Weight at every visit. Menstrual history. Bone health long-term. TDM: 50–125 μg/ml; ≈100 μg/ml in acute mania. 24-hour post-dose sample. Routine TDM not required if responding and tolerated.
Baseline: LFT, FBC, sodium (baseline and at 1 month — hyponatraemia), ECG, OCP review (CYP3A4 induction), HLA-B*15:02 in Asian descent. Ongoing: Level, LFT, FBC every 3 months in first year then 6-monthly. Sodium annually. OCP failure warning. TDM: 4–12 μg/mL; in BPAD often 8–12 μg/mL. Sample 12 hours post-dose at steady state.
Safety measure is the slow titration protocol: 25 mg/day × 2 weeks → 50 mg/day × 2 weeks → 100 mg/day → 200 mg target. This reduces risk of Stevens-Johnson syndrome and toxic epidermal necrolysis. Stop at first sign of rash. No clinically useful TDM range.
Sodium baseline required. Sodium at 1 month then 6-monthly — hyponatraemia is commoner than with carbamazepine. No clinically useful TDM range. Not routinely measured.

D2 Antipsychotics

▶
Baseline: Weight, waist, BMI, BP, fasting glucose, HbA1c, fasting lipids, prolactin (if symptomatic), ECG (QTc), LFT. Ongoing: Weight, BP every visit. Fasting glucose, HbA1c, lipids at 12 weeks then 6-monthly. ECG annually or if symptomatic. TDM: 20–80 ng/mL; reserve for non-response or suspected non-adherence.
ECG (QTc) baseline mandatory — dose-dependent QTc prolongation. ECG repeat at therapeutic dose and annually. Metabolic panel as olanzapine.
Prolactin baseline mandatory — risperidone is the most prolactin-elevating SGA at therapeutic doses. Prolactin at 3 months and if symptomatic (galactorrhoea, amenorrhoea, sexual dysfunction, gynaecomastia). Metabolic panel as olanzapine. Not routinely measured for TDM.
Baseline: CBC with ANC ≥3.0 × 10^9/L mandatory, ECG, ECHO, troponin (myocarditis baseline, risk window is first 6–8 weeks), LFT, fasting glucose, HbA1c, fasting lipids, weight, BMI, BP. Ongoing: CBC weekly × 18 weeks → fortnightly to 1 year → 4-weekly indefinitely. Stop if ANC <1.5; immediate stop if <1.0. Troponin + ECHO baseline + at 1 month. CRP weekly × 4 weeks (myocarditis screen). Metabolic panel every 6 months. Weight every visit. Constipation review — clozapine ileus is fatal. TDM: Plasma clozapine 350–600 ng/mL (non-response/breakthrough). <350 = under-dosing; >600 = seizure risk. Norclozapine ratio for compliance.
ECG (QTc) baseline mandatory. EPS examination at every visit. AIMS quarterly for tardive dyskinesia. QTc if dose >10 mg/day or polypharmacy with other QT-prolonging agents. Not routinely measured for TDM.
As for the parent oral drug. ANC and metabolic panel as for the oral drug. Injection-site review. Titration to optimal dose may take months.

D3 Antidepressants

▶
Baseline: None mandatory in young healthy patients. Sodium baseline if elderly (SIADH risk). LFT if hepatic disease. ECG if citalopram (QTc — not available in India). Ongoing: Sodium screen if symptomatic in elderly. PHQ-9 at week 4, 8, 12. Sexual side-effect enquiry at every visit. Bleeding risk warning if on anticoagulants or NSAIDs. Response rule: PHQ-9 ≥25% reduction at week 4 = continue; <25% = increase or switch (IPS-CPG 2026 algorithm).
BP baseline mandatory for venlafaxine — dose-dependent hypertension. BP at every visit, especially venlafaxine >150 mg/day. LFT for duloxetine at 3 months. Discontinuation syndrome: taper slowly.
Baseline: Weight, lipids, fasting glucose. Follow-up at 6 months: weight and lipids. FBC if neutropenia symptoms (rare). Sedation and appetite gain are leveraged when clinically desired.
Seizure history screen mandatory — contraindicated in any seizure disorder, eating disorder with electrolyte disturbance, or alcohol withdrawal. BP if hypertension. Weight (often weight-neutral or weight-loss).
Baseline: ECG mandatory (QRS widening, QTc, conduction blocks). BP supine and standing. Glucose. FBC. Ongoing: ECG at therapeutic dose. Postural BP every visit. Constipation, urinary retention, dry mouth, blurred vision review. TDM available for nortriptyline (50–150 ng/mL) and imipramine + desipramine (150–300 ng/mL) — useful in non-response or toxicity.
Moclobemide is a reversible MAO-A inhibitor — the only Indian-available MAOI. No dietary restriction required (unlike non-selective MAOIs). Baseline: LFT, BP. Ongoing: BP at every visit. Serotonin syndrome warning if combined with serotonergic drug.

D4 Cognitive Enhancers (Dementia)

▶
Baseline: ECG (bradycardia, heart block — cholinesterase inhibitors are vagotonic), pulse, BP, weight. Ongoing: ECG and weight 6-monthly. Pulse at every visit. Cholinergic side-effect review: nausea, diarrhoea, vivid dreams, urinary frequency.
Baseline: RFT (renal excretion, dose adjust if eGFR <30). Ongoing: RFT annually. Combination with cholinesterase inhibitor allowed and often used.

D5 Anti-Craving & Addiction Medications

▶
Baseline: LFT. Ongoing: LFT at 3 months then 6-monthly. Opioid screen must be clear before initiation — precipitated opioid withdrawal can be severe and dangerous. Depot naltrexone: injection-site monitoring, compliance confirmation, relapse-risk monitoring.
Baseline: RFT (renal excretion, contraindicated if severe renal impairment). Ongoing: RFT annually. Use in motivated patients with goal of abstinence; not compatible with controlled drinking.
Baseline: LFT (can cause hepatotoxicity). Cardiac history: severe IHD is a contraindication (metabolite acetaldehyde causes systemic vasodilation, hypotension, chest pain). Patient consent + supervisor ID mandatory. Disulfiram-ethanol reaction warning. Review LFT at 3 months then 6-monthly.
Baseline: LFT, ECG (methadone: QTc baseline mandatory — dose-dependent QTc prolongation), urine toxicology, FBC. Ongoing: Random urine toxicology. LFT 6-monthly. QTc if dose >100 mg/day methadone.
FDA black-box neuropsychiatric warning. Baseline: Psychiatric history review, mood and behaviour review, suicide screen. Monitor: Mood, behaviour, suicide risk. Not routinely measured for TDM. Not available in India as of 2024.

D6 Stimulants & ADHD Agents

▶
Baseline: BP, pulse, weight, height, cardiac history, family history of sudden cardiac death. Ongoing: BP, pulse, weight, height every 3 months. BP if dose >60 mg/day. TDM: Not routinely measured. Rebound / tics review at every visit. Growth chart in children.
Baseline: LFT, BP, pulse. Ongoing: LFT at 3 months then 6-monthly. BP, pulse at every visit. Suicidal ideation review (FDA black-box in adolescents). Not routinely measured for TDM.

D7 Anxiolytics & Sedatives

▶
Substance use history reviewed. Falls risk assessed in elderly (FDA boxed warning). Tolerance and dependence monitoring at every visit. Time-limited use — recommend ≤2–4 weeks outside specific indications (catatonia, alcohol withdrawal, acute agitation).
Same monitoring as benzodiazepines. Falls risk in elderly. Tolerance and dependence. Time-limited use. Not suitable for chronic insomnia.
Baseline: RFT (renal excretion — dose adjustment required). Ongoing: RFT annually. Pregabalin abuse-liability monitoring at every visit. TDM: Not routinely measured. Not available for anxiety as licensed indication.

D8 ECT Pre-Treatment Workup

▶
In addition to the baseline psychiatric workup: ECG, chest X-ray, CBC, RFT, LFT, electrolytes, fasting glucose, INR if on anticoagulants, anaesthetic clearance, dental review (loose teeth), spinal X-ray if osteoporosis suspected, MRI brain if cardiac pacemaker (relative contraindication — MRI compatibility check), pregnancy test (mandatory in women of reproductive age). Thiamine empirical if alcohol-related malnutrition.
Track every session: seizure duration, EEG seizure adequacy (must exceed 15 seconds), post-ictal confusion, cognitive side-effects, cardiac rhythm, any adverse events. Use Right Unilateral ECT first if cognitive side-effect concern; switch to bilateral if inadequate response after 6 sessions.

F Pharmacogenomic Testing

▶

When Pharmacogenomics Matters

  • Narrowly indicated, expensive, and rarely changes a single decision in routine Indian practice.
  • Three specific scenarios are highly relevant and increasingly examinable.
  • The candidate is expected to know: name the allele, the population frequency, the drug, and the action.

F1 HLA-B*15:02 — Carbamazepine in Asian Populations

▶
Most actionable pharmacogenomic test in Indian psychiatry. Variant HLA-B*15:02 present in 2–8% of Indian populations. Risk: ~100-fold elevated SJS/TEN with carbamazepine. FDA, EMA, IPS recommend testing before carbamazepine in Asian descent. Cost ~₹2500–4000; turnaround 3–7 days. Action: Positive = hard contraindication. Use valproate, lamotrigine, or another agent. Risk also extends to oxcarbazepine (lower but real) and phenytoin (HLA-B*15:02 + HLA-B*15:11).

F2 CYP2D6 / CYP2C19 — ADs & Antipsychotics

▶
Four phenotypes: Poor Metaboliser (PM — drug accumulates, side effects at standard doses), Intermediate (IM — reduced clearance, dose adjustment), Normal (NM — standard dosing), Ultra-Rapid (UM — rapid clearance, pseudo non-responder). CYP2D6 metabolises fluoxetine, paroxetine, venlafaxine, duloxetine, vortioxetine, risperidone, aripiprazole, haloperidol, clomipramine, amitriptyline, nortriptyline, atomoxetine. CYP2C19 metabolises citalopram, escitalopram, sertraline, clomipramine, amitriptyline, imipramine, diazepam.

G Pearls & Pitfalls

▶

Common Pitfalls in Investigation Planning

  • Do not order EEG for non-specific reasons — low yield in routine psychiatric workup.
  • MRI > CT for psychiatric workup — CT misses small lesions, white matter changes, mesial temporal pathology.
  • Do not repeat investigations needlessly — ask "what has changed?" before re-ordering.
  • Pre-clozapine ECHO and troponin baseline — myocarditis risk highest in first 6–8 weeks.

H Sample Documentation Templates

▶

How to Document in the Case Sheet

  • Use these stems to structure your viva answer and case-sheet documentation.
  • Each stem follows the graded-answer format: baseline + targeted with justification.
  • Adapt the text in the fields below to your specific patient.

H1 Generic Baseline Panel Stem

▶
Start with this sentence and adapt it. The examiner expects to hear this structure first: the baseline panel that any patient with this presentation would receive, independent of specific patient features.

H2 Targeted Addition Stem

▶
Add one or two targeted tests with explicit justification. This is what separates a pass from a distinction. The justification must be tied to a specific patient feature, not a generic "to rule out."

H3 Lithium Pre-Initiation Stem

▶
Viva-ready lithium monitoring stem. Must include: baseline panel, day-5 level, frequency schedule, therapeutic target ranges, and what to do if eGFR or TSH deteriorates.

H4 Clozapine Pre-Initiation Stem

▶
The most intensive monitoring in psychiatry. Must include: CBC with ANC threshold, ECHO/troponin baseline + 1 month, CRP weekly x 4, metabolic panel, weight, clozapine level target, constipation review, and the NORC registry.

H5 Autoimmune Encephalitis Red-Flag Stem

▶
The autoimmune encephalitis stem is increasingly examinable. Must mention: serum anti-NMDA-R, anti-VGKC (LGI1, CASPR2), anti-GAD65, paraneoplastic panel, CSF if serum positive, MRI brain, EEG, and neurology referral.

H6 HLA-B*15:02 Stem

▶
This is the most actionable pharmacogenomic test in Indian psychiatry. Must state: population frequency (2–8%), risk magnitude (~100-fold), cost (~₹3000), turnaround (~1 week), and action on positive (switch drug).

H7 Investigations Refusal Stem (FND/BPD/Dissociative)

▶
This is a scored element. The examiner wants to hear that you considered investigations, decided they were not indicated, and documented that decision explicitly. Over-investigation risks iatrogenic harm and reinforcement of medical-model framing.

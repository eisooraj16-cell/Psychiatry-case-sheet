Primary vs Secondary Delusions

Primary (apophany): delusional mood, sudden delusional idea, delusional perception.

Secondary: arises from depressed mood, hallucinations, or stress.

Clinical Instruction

The MSE is a cross-sectional record anchored in the last 7 days. Three rules govern everything:

  • Describe, don't label. Document heading → verbatim → interpretation as three distinct entities.
  • Patient's language first. Record thought, perception, affect, mood, and insight verbatim. Mark translations as "translated".
  • Time and date the MSE. Without timestamping, it is not interpretable.

Clinical Instruction

Disorders of form ask: is the architecture of thinking intact? Sample sufficient length of speech (spontaneous or to an open, non-emotionally-charged cue) before commenting.

Pitfall: Do not confuse flight of ideas with derailment. FOI is fast but connections are understandable; derailment loses the connections. Tangentiality never reaches the goal; circumstantiality always does.

Definitions (Fish)
  • Derailment: Ideas slip onto another track, obliquely related or unrelated. FOI = derailment occurring rapidly in pressured speech.
  • Tangentiality: Response stays on subject with slight deviation each time but eventually goes completely off topic. Distinguished from circumstantiality (which always returns to goal).
  • Substitution: One major thought replaced by a subsidiary one.
  • Desultory thinking: Speech grammatically correct but sudden ideas force their way in.
  • Omission: Intention interrupted leaving a gap.
  • Drivelling: Preliminary outline present but organisation lost, parts get muddled.
  • Fusion: Heterogeneous elements brought together with broken logical progression.
  • Word salad: Terminal disorganisation; words without recognisable syntactic links.
Quote patient speech showing the form disturbance. Include the cue/question you gave.

Clinical Instruction

Stream assesses tempo and continuity of thinking. Do not confuse thought blocking (sudden arrest, new thought emerges) with poverty of thought (generalised slowing). Poverty hampers formation of associations; blocking is an abrupt stop.

Tempo Definitions
  • Pressure / push of speech / flight of ideas: Thoughts follow rapidly with no general direction; connections due to chance factors (clang, punning, distraction) but usually understandable.
  • Poverty of thought: Slowing hampers formation of associations and may prevent reaching the original goal.
  • Circumstantiality: Slow, with many unnecessary trivial details, but goal never lost and eventually reached.
Continuity Definitions
  • Perseveration: Mental operations persist beyond point of relevance. Asked previous PM replies "Manmohan Singh"; asked present PM, again "Manmohan Singh".
  • Thought blocking: Sudden arrest of train of thought leaving a 'blank'. An entirely new thought may then begin.
Document the verbatim speech showing the stream disturbance, with the cue given.

Clinical Instruction

Examine for overvalued ideas and delusions, then preoccupations, suicidal ideation, depressive cognitions, and substance-use motivational stage. A delusion is a false, unshakeable belief out of keeping with the patient's social and cultural background [Fish].

Apply the cultural test before labelling any belief as delusional. A religiously-sanctioned belief in keeping with social/cultural background is NOT a delusion.

Clinical Instruction

In organic brain disease, time is lost first, then place, then person. Recovery is in reverse: person → place → time. This single rule helps stage delirium and distinguish it from functional psychosis where orientation is typically preserved.

Stupor = reversible unresponsiveness requiring stimulus to maintain awakeness. Drowsiness = reversible to alert by stimulus.

OPCRAST & Key Mnemonics

▾

OPCRAST — For the index HOPI presentation: Onset, Precipitant, Course, Risk (current), Associated disturbances (biological functions + functional impact), Symptoms in chronological order, Treatment received this episode.

Biological sweep order: Sleep → Appetite → Weight → Energy → Libido → Diurnal variation → Bowel/bladder

Suicide screen (Patterson ladder): 1. Life not worth living → 2. Wish not to wake → 3. Thoughts of doing something → 4. Plan → 5. Steps taken → 6. What stops you (protective factors)

A NIMHANS Framework

▾

Why this section matters

The NIMHANS proforma is the backbone of the HOPI. A detailed, coherent account from onset to consultation with chronological evolution is required. The first six items are verbatim from the proforma; the four extensions are mandated by IPS Karnataka 2023 consensus.

A1. Mode of Onset

Diagnostic significance

  • Abrupt/acute onset → organic, delirium, drug-induced psychosis, autoimmune encephalitis, post-ictal, brief psychotic disorder, mania, substance-related.
  • Insidious onset → schizophrenia, recurrent depression, dementia, personality decompensation.
  • The taxonomy is exam-defensible: timeline is your first defence of the differential.
Sample phrasing
“Start from the last time you felt fully like yourself, and walk me forward.”
“What was the last day you felt fully like yourself? Not perfect, just yourself. The version your family would recognise.”

Exam pearl: If patient and family cannot name “the last day fully yourself”, onset is by definition insidious.

Describe exactly how the illness began, in the patient’s or family’s own words. This is the anchor of the entire HOPI narrative.
Common pitfall

Patient and family under-report the prodrome in schizophrenia and depression because it was unremarkable in retrospect. The “last day fully yourself” anchor is the corrective.

Any pre-morbid changes: social withdrawal, dropping grades, sleep-wake reversal, suspiciousness, irritability. Document with approximate dates.
A2. Precipitating Factors

How it shifts the differential

  • Precipitants are not causes. Use the diathesis-stress framework.
  • Always ascertain: did the event precede the illness or was it a consequence?
  • Job loss after illness onset is a marker, not a trigger.
Documentation rule

Write: “The precipitant for the index episode was [X], on a background of [diathesis].” Never write: “caused by [X].”

“The job loss occurred 2 weeks after mood symptoms began; it is therefore a marker of functional impact, not a trigger.”
Describe the event, date, and whether it preceded or followed symptom onset. Always state the temporal direction: did the stressor come before the first symptom, or after?
A3. Course of Illness

What to document

  • Episodic, continuous, or fluctuating.
  • Has the symptom phenotype evolved? (e.g., positive symptoms → apathy/blunting in later schizophrenia).
  • Consider a graphic life chart if useful (cross-reference Chapter 05).
Describe the pattern of illness, any periods of recovery, and whether symptom type has changed over time. Note if positive symptoms evolved into negative symptoms.
A4. Symptoms in Chronological Order

Why chronology is the temporal architecture of diagnosis

  • What came first: mood change or psychosis? Substance use or depression? Sleep disturbance or suicidal thinking?
  • Sequence distinguishes primary mood disorder with secondary psychotic features vs. primary psychotic illness with secondary mood features.
  • This distinction reshapes the entire treatment plan.

Exam trap

  • Patients reflexively start with what is most distressing today. Your job is to anchor them to the first day and walk forward. A backward-built timeline is almost always wrong.
How to document

Write as a timeline: Week 0 → sleep change; Week 2 → low mood; Week 4 → anhedonia; Week 6 → passive SI; Week 8 → active SI with plan.

Viva tip: Lead with onset, course, and the symptom that defines the index episode. Then build forward in time.

List each symptom with its approximate date relative to onset, in forward chronological order. What came first, what followed, and how did the constellation build up over time?
A5. Associated Disturbances

Biological functions + functional impact cluster

The specific nature of the disturbance and the degree of disability must both be recorded. Ask in order: Sleep → Appetite → Weight → Energy → Libido → Diurnal variation → Bowel/bladder.

Sleep
Manic disambiguation

Sleeps 3 hours and feels rested → reduced need for sleep (manic).

Sleeps 3 hours and feels exhausted → insomnia (depressive/anxious).

“How has your sleep been? How long are you sleeping? When you wake up, do you feel rested or exhausted?”
Document sleep quality, daytime fatigue, naps, any sleep aids used. Include the manic disambiguator: does the patient feel rested or exhausted on waking?
Appetite & Weight
Describe nature of change, any compensatory behaviours (purging, excessive exercise), BMI trajectory if relevant. For anorexia, note whether hunger is preserved but actively overridden (discriminator from depression).
Energy & Libido
Diurnal Variation & Other
Bowel changes, urinary symptoms, menstrual changes (if applicable), other somatic symptoms. Ask about constipation, cold intolerance, diaphoresis.
Functional Impact (Social & Occupational)
Scoring functional impact

Mild = some reduction in performance; Moderate = significant reduction but still attending; Severe = unable to attend work/college; Total = completely stopped.

Severity is the functional impairment the symptoms cause, not the symptom count alone.

Describe specific impact on occupation, education, relationships, self-care, finances. Include number of days absent from work/college if applicable.
A6. Organic Screen

Why screen everyone

NIMHANS notes these are particularly important in the elderly but should be asked of every patient. The index episode may be the first presentation of an organic illness.

List current or past physical illnesses with relevance to psychiatric presentation. Include medications currently taken for physical conditions.
Screen for alcohol, tobacco, cannabis, opioids, stimulants, benzodiazepines, OTCs. Document quantity/frequency if applicable.
A7. Treatment Received During This Episode

Common pitfall

  • Not asking about informal, AYUSH, or faith-based treatment. In Indian patients these are the rule, not the exception.
  • Drug interactions (St John’s Wort with SSRIs, heavy metals in Ayurvedic preparations, Datura anticholinergic ingestions) are common and missed.
What to document for each agent

Drug name, dose, duration, response (partial / full / none), adherence (good / partial / poor), side effects. Include psychotherapy, ECT, hospitalisation.

“Before you came here, did you see anyone else for this — a doctor, a homeopath, a vaidya, a faith healer? Did you take anything for it — tablets, drops, oils?”
For each drug: name | dose | duration | response (partial/full/none) | adherence (good/partial/poor) | side effects. Include start and stop dates.
Describe any non-allopathic treatment, practitioner details, substances taken, duration, perceived response. Ask specifically about faith healers, homeopaths, Ayurvedic practitioners, and family remedies.
Document type, number of sessions/days, dates, and response. Note involuntary admissions and MHCA sections if applicable.
A8. Current Risk Assessment

Current risk only

Past attempts and deliberate self-harm belong in Chapter 05 (Past History). This section captures current suicide/self-harm, harm to others, self-neglect, and wandering risk.

Documentation trap

  • Documenting “no SI” without documenting how it was asked is unsafe documentation. Write the laddered question and answer verbatim where possible.
Laddered questions (ask in order, document each)
1. “Has it ever felt like life isn’t worth living?”
2. “Have you wished you wouldn’t wake up?”
3. “Have you thought of actually doing something to end your life?”
4. “Have you thought about how you would do it?”
5. “Have you taken any steps towards it — bought anything, written anything, said goodbye?”
6. “What stops you?” (protective factors)
Document EACH question asked and the answer given verbatim. Do not write “no SI” without the question. Include the final question about protective factors.
“Has there been any time in this illness when you’ve felt like hurting someone, or felt that someone needed to be hurt because of what they were doing to you?”
Document the exact question asked and the verbatim answer. Note any delusion-driven accusation or command hallucination content.
Summarise current risk level, protective factors (family support, religious beliefs, children), and immediate safety plan if applicable. Include capacity for safe discharge if relevant.
A9. Legal Issues Related to This Episode
Document dates, sections invoked, current legal status, court dates, and any capacity assessment outcomes.
A10. Severity Rating

CGI-S + scale-based instruments

Conflating severity with symptom count is a common error. Severity is the functional impairment the symptoms cause, scored on CGI or a validated instrument.

NormalBorderlineMildModerateMarkedSevereExtreme
Enter scores, dates of administration, and any scale-specific observations. Note pre- and post-treatment scores if available.

B Why It Matters (Diagnostic Engine)

▾

The HOPI is the diagnostic engine of the case

The differential is built here, the formulation hinges here, and the management plan in Chapter 14 is downstream of decisions made in this chapter, or mistakes made in it.

Your leading differential based on onset, course, and chronology. State with confidence level.
Alternative diagnoses with supporting and refuting features from HOPI. What findings argue against each alternative?
Which features in this HOPI point toward one diagnosis over another? Focus on temporal sequence, biological markers, and treatment response.
Document the pre-existing vulnerability and the stressor interplay. Remember: precipitants are NOT causes. Frame as “the precipitant was [X], on a background of [diathesis].”

C How to Elicit (Interview Phases)

▾
C1. Free Narrative Phase (3–5 min, uninterrupted)

Why silence is data

  • The patient is telling you what they think the problem is, in the order they think it occurred. That order is itself diagnostic information.
  • Resist the urge to clarify, summarise, or redirect.
  • Use only minimal continuers: “mm”, “go on”, a nod.
Summarise the uninterrupted narrative as the patient told it, preserving their language and order. This is the raw data from which the HOPI is built.
C2. Systematic Funnelling Phase

The funnel structure

For each symptom domain mentioned: Open → Focused → Closed. This converts a vague complaint into a documentable, scoreable, time-stamped phenomenon.

For each symptom: open question used → focused questions → closed quantification (date, frequency, severity, triggers). Document in the order funnelled.
C3. Completion Phase — Review of Psychiatric Systems

Why completion is mandatory

  • Patients leave out symptoms that are ego-syntonic, shameful, illegal, or that they have come to consider normal.
  • A “no” is documented as a no — this is the bridge to Chapter 04 (Negative History).
Standard sweep domains

Mood (depression, mania, anxiety, irritability) → Psychotic phenomena (hallucinations, delusions, thought possession) → OCD phenomena → Trauma and PTSD phenomena → Eating, weight, body image → Substance use (alcohol, tobacco, cannabis, opioids, stimulants, benzodiazepines, OTCs) → Suicide and self-harm → Harm to others → Sleep → Appetite → Weight → Energy → Libido → Cognitive symptoms.

Every one gets a screening question regardless of presentation.

Document each domain, the screening question asked, and the response (positive or negative). Write negative findings as “screened, denied” not just “no.”
C4. Mode-of-Onset Elicitation
Patient’s exact response to “What was the last day you felt fully like yourself?” If patient cannot answer, document family response. If neither can answer, onset is insidious by definition.
C5. Biological Functions Micro-Funnel

Order: Sleep → Appetite → Weight → Energy → Libido → Diurnal variation → Bowel/bladder

Sleep is the highest-yield. The manic disambiguator: “Do you feel rested or exhausted?”

Write as a single fluent paragraph in viva-ready prose. Include the manic disambiguator for sleep, and ask about diurnal variation explicitly.
C6. Risk Micro-Funnel (Patterson Framework)

Asked late, after rapport is established, and asked directly

Move from passive ideation → active ideation → plan → means → intent → rehearsal → protective factors. Document the question asked and the answer given, verbatim.

Laddered questions and verbatim answers, plus protective factors. Include the question AND the answer for each step. Never write “denied SI” without the question.
C7. Collateral Micro-Protocol

Missing collateral is a critical error

  • In psychosis, mania, and neurocognitive cases the patient cannot give the HOPI alone.
  • Interview the informant separately on at least 3 points: (1) timeline of onset, (2) behavioural change at home, (3) any risk events not disclosed.
(1) Timeline confirmation or discrepancy, (2) Behavioural changes observed at home, (3) Risk events not disclosed by patient. Document verbatim quotes where possible.
C8. Transition Phrases Out of HOPI
“Before this episode began, has there been any time in your life when you’ve felt anything similar, even if much milder?”
Document patient’s response to transition into past history. Note any prior episodes even if milder.
“Has anyone in your family ever had anything like what you are going through, or any other mental health condition?”
Document response and note which relatives, what conditions, and treatment history if volunteered.
Speech (rate, volume, pressure), thought form (linear, tangential, circumstantial), affect (range, congruence), psychomotor activity (retardation, agitation) observed throughout the interview.

Framing note

In BPD the “episode” framing breaks down. The HOPI captures the decompensation against the chronic baseline. Say this explicitly in viva: “This is a case of chronic dysregulation with acute decompensation, not a discrete episode in the manic-or-depressive sense.”

D11. Neurocognitive — Delirium vs. Dementia
Single most important question

Acuity of onset determines delirium vs dementia. Delirium = abrupt (hours–days), fluctuating, sundowning, visual hallucinations, impaired attention. Dementia = insidious (months–years), progressive, clear consciousness.

E Pitfalls & Exam Traps

▾

1. Confusing HOPI with Past History

  • The index episode lives here. Prior episodes, even closely preceding ones, live in Chapter 05.
  • Boundary: the last clean recovery (ISBD Tohen 2009 vocabulary). If no recovery between previous and current, they are part of the same continuous illness and belong together in HOPI.

2. Letting the patient set the chronology backward

  • Patients reflexively start with what is most distressing today.
  • Anchor them to the first day and walk forward. A backward-built timeline is almost always wrong.

3. Calling a precipitant a cause

  • The diathesis-stress trap. Examiners will challenge “his depression was caused by job loss.”
  • Reframe: “The precipitant for the index episode was job loss, on a background of [diathesis].”

4. Missing the prodrome

  • Especially in schizophrenia and depression, patient and family under-report the prodrome.
  • The “last day fully yourself” anchor is the corrective.

5. Skipping the biological functions cluster

  • Sleep, appetite, weight, energy, libido, and diurnal variation must be asked in every HOPI regardless of presentation.
  • They are diagnostically discriminating, scoreable, and trackable across treatment.

6. Documenting “no SI” without the question

  • A risk denial is meaningful only if the question was asked.
  • Write the laddered question and the answer, verbatim where possible.

7. Missing collateral when the patient lacks insight

  • In psychosis, mania, and neurocognitive cases the patient cannot give the HOPI.
  • The candidate who tries to construct it from the patient alone produces an unusable case.

8. Not asking about informal, AYUSH, or faith-based treatment

  • In Indian patients these are the rule, not the exception.
  • Drug interactions (St John’s Wort + SSRIs, heavy metals, anticholinergic Datura) are common and missed.

9. Conflating severity with symptom count

  • Severity is the functional impairment the symptoms cause, scored on CGI or validated instrument.
  • Not the number of symptoms ticked.

10. Forgetting treatment-emergent affective switching

  • A patient who had hypomania while on an SSRI in the index episode is a different case.
  • Cross-reference Chapter 05 for formal definition and diagnostic implications.

Mood Probe
English: “How has your mood been over the last few weeks?”
Hindi: “pichhle kuch hafton mein aapka mann kaisa raha hai?”
Sleep Probe (with manic disambiguation)
“How has your sleep been? How long are you sleeping at night? When you wake up, do you feel rested, or do you feel exhausted?”
Disambiguator: sleeps 4 hours and feels rested = reduced need (manic); sleeps 4 hours and exhausted = insomnia (depressive/anxious).
Biological Functions Sweep
“How has your appetite been? Has your weight changed? How is your energy through the day? Has there been any change in your interest in physical intimacy? Is there any time of day when you feel better or worse?”

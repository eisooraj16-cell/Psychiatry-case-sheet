Pitfalls & Exam Traps (§F) — Checklist
Skipping informant reliability. The entire longitudinal history rests on the informant.
Conflating current age with age of illness onset. Current age lives in Demographics; illness onset lives in HOPI (Ch03).
Missing occupation-specific risks. Shift work, healthcare, military, farming pesticides, CSW, migrant labour, student status.
Ignoring language mismatch. English-only candidate with Kannada patient through family interpreter = filtered interview.
Using English cognitive instrument in non-English-literate patient. Use HMSE (Ganguli 1995) not MMSE/MoCA.
Documenting "self-referred" without asking about prior contacts. Many have seen GPs or traditional practitioners.
Defaulting to binary gender slip. Missing hijra and trans-identified patients has downstream consequences.
Treating the demographic slip as a formality. It is the first differential-shifting evidence of the case.
A1
Name & Preferred Address
Full name as patient gives it; anonymise to initials/code (PT-XXX)
Clinical note: Record the full name as the patient gives it. The preferred address is asked explicitly and used throughout the interview. Anonymise to initials or PT-XXX in documents leaving the clinical setting.
Patient's full name as she gives it. Use anonymised code (e.g., PT-001) in any document that leaves the clinical setting. The preferred address is asked explicitly and used throughout the interview.
Ask explicitly: "How would you like me to address you?" Use this form throughout the interview. In Indian practice, patients may prefer cultural forms (Bhaiya, Didi) over Western honorifics.
A2
Age
Single largest prior-shifting item in the demographic slip
Critical distinction: This is the patient's current age at interview, NOT age at onset of illness, NOT age at first psychiatric contact, NOT age at first hospitalisation. Those live in HOPI (Ch03), past history (Ch05), and treatment history respectively.
Age is the single strongest prior-shifting item. A first-episode psychosis at 19 is a different case from one at 65. Check the Age-of-Onset table above for reference.
Enter age in completed years. If the patient does not know their exact date of birth, estimate and flag as approximate. In Indian rural practice, stated age is sometimes inaccurate in older patients.
Flag estimated ages and note any relevant age-of-onset considerations here. Document your reasoning if age is estimated rather than exact.
A3
Sex & Gender Identity
NALSA 2014 & Transgender Act 2019 — three-gender recognition
Legal context: Indian law recognises three genders: male, female, and third gender (hijra, kinnar, transgender) per NALSA 2014 and Transgender Persons Act 2019. Patients from the hijra community face elevated rates of depression, self-harm, substance use, and HIV.
Clinical relevance: Sex-specific epidemiology shifts the differential (anorexia nervosa, BPD more prevalent in women; AUD, ASPD more prevalent in men). Gender dysphoria and trans-identity are increasingly recognised clinical presentations.
Sample phrasing for gender identity inquiry
When relevant
"I'd like to ask how you identify, I ask everyone, and I want to make sure I'm addressing you correctly. What pronouns do you use?"
Document separately when it differs from sex assigned at birth. Use patient's chosen pronouns throughout the interview. The Indian legal context recognises third gender as an explicit option.
A4
Education
Highest level completed; component of Kuppuswamy SES scoring
Three clinical jobs of education: (1) Component of Kuppuswamy SES calculation, (2) Proxy for cognitive reserve — higher education slows apparent dementia decline, (3) Determines cognitive instrument choice: English MMSE/MoCA is invalid for non-English-literate patients; use Hindi Mental Status Examination (HMSE, Ganguli 1995) instead.
Maps onto Kuppuswamy education scoring (Chapter 06 §A8). Select the highest completed level. Education score auto-flows into the Kuppuswamy SES calculator in Section A8.
Note language of literacy (English vs regional), any cognitive instrument implications, or ongoing education that may be relevant to the clinical picture.
A5
Occupation
Current occupation; certain occupations carry specific psychiatric risks
Occupation-specific psychiatric risks (expand to review)
  • Shift workers: Circadian disruption, mood and sleep disorders, substance use as self-management
  • Healthcare workers: Elevated rates of depression, suicide, burnout, SUD; access to opioids/sedatives
  • Military and police: Trauma exposure, PTSD, AUD; access to firearms in suicide risk assessment
  • Farmers: Organophosphate exposure (cholinergic syndromes), pesticide self-poisoning, economic distress suicide
  • Commercial sex workers: Elevated rates of depression, PTSD, substance use, HIV; stigma-related barriers
  • Migrant labourers: Isolation, alcohol use, remittance stress, separation from spouse/children
  • Students: Examination-related anxiety, suicide around results, substance use, first-episode psychosis age window
Record current occupation. Ask about prior occupations if current is short-standing. Certain occupations carry specific psychiatric risks that shift the differential at intake (see expandable panel above).
Select the category that best matches the patient's occupation for Kuppuswamy SES calculation. The occupation score auto-flows into Section A8.
Document prior occupations if current is short-standing, and any significant periods of unemployment with durations. Occupational trajectory is relevant for functional assessment.
A6
Marital Status
Current status and duration; never-married adults in 30s-40s may signal schizophrenia
Diagnostic relevance: Never-married adults in their 30s-40s with limited social function shifts prior toward schizophrenia spectrum or severe mood disorder. Recently widowed patients present with bereavement-related disorders. Separated/divorced with recent onset shifts toward adjustment, depressive, and BPD presentations. The detailed marital history lives in Chapter 07 (§A9).
Record the current marital status. Ask about duration of current status. This item carries diagnostic relevance: prolonged never-married status in middle age is a soft sign for schizophrenia spectrum disorders.
Ask how long the patient has been in this marital status. Recent changes (e.g., widowed 2 months ago) carry different diagnostic weight than long-standing states.
A7
Religion
Affiliation by birth and current practice; context for cultural formulation
Clinical relevance: Religion provides context for cultural formulation, shapes help-seeking trajectory (Chapter 07 §A12), and is relevant to specific clinical content: scrupulosity in OCD, religious delusions in psychosis, religious coping in bereavement. Keep brief — four or five words in the final slip.
The religion the patient was born into. This provides the cultural baseline for the cultural formulation.
Note if the patient has converted, is non-practicing, or follows a different spiritual path than their birth religion.

A9
Residence
Urban residence elevates prior for schizophrenia; rural = organophosphate risk
Urban residence elevates the prior for schizophrenia at population level. Rural residence carries organophosphate suicide risk and specific cultural presentations. Migration is itself a stressor with elevated risk for psychosis.
Document explicitly because presentation patterns, help-seeking pathways, and access barriers differ substantially between urban, semi-urban, and rural settings.
The region of origin where it differs from current residence. Relevant for cultural formulation and migration history.
If the patient has migrated, document when, why, and the duration of the current stay. Migration is itself a stressor and carries elevated risk for several psychiatric conditions, particularly psychosis (the urbanicity and migration findings from schizophrenia epidemiology literature).
A10
Language
Interview language determines cognitive instrument choice
In multilingual Indian settings, the candidate often conducts the interview in one language while documenting in another. The translation step is itself a source of clinical information loss — flag this where relevant. Interview language determines cognitive instrument choice (MMSE vs HMSE).
Sample phrasing for language selection
Language of interview
"Which language would you be most comfortable speaking in today? Kannada, Hindi, English, or something else? We can switch between languages as we go if that helps."
List all languages the patient speaks, in order of fluency. The first-listed language is the primary language of communication.
The language in which the clinical interview was conducted. This determines which cognitive screening instrument is valid (e.g., English MMSE vs Hindi HMSE).
Document who served as interpreter. Family-member interpreters may filter sensitive content. Professional or hospital interpreters are preferred for clinical accuracy.
A11
Informant Details & Reliability
The entire longitudinal history rests on the informant's quality
Critical: Skipping the informant reliability assessment is a major pitfall. The entire longitudinal history rests on the informant. When no informant is available, document this explicitly as a limitation — "no informant available; history is from the patient alone, with the expected limitations of self-report."
Use a descriptor rather than the actual name for privacy (e.g., "husband", "elder son"). Anonymise as you would the patient name.
An informant who has known the patient for 20 years is structurally better positioned than one who has known her for 3 months. Duration of acquaintance is the first item of the 3-item reliability quick test.
An informant who has not seen the patient in 2 months cannot provide a reliable account of the last 2 weeks. This is the second item of the reliability quick test.
3-item informant reliability quick test (§C2)

The reliability of the informant is a clinical judgement based on these three items:

  • Duration of acquaintance: Longer acquaintance = structurally better positioned
  • Current contact frequency: Daily contact can speak to current illness; monthly cannot describe last 2 weeks reliably
  • Internal consistency: Does the informant's spontaneous account match answers to directed questions and match the patient's account on plausible points?
The third item of the reliability quick test. Consistency raises reliability; contradiction lowers it. This is a clinical judgement, not a scored instrument.
Rate based on the three-item quick test above. Document a brief justifying phrase alongside the rating.
Provide a brief justifying phrase that covers the three items: duration, contact frequency, and internal consistency.
A12
Source of Referral
Shapes expectations and is itself clinically informative
A police-brought patient, a court-mandated assessment, and a self-referred patient are three very different clinical contexts. The management plan must acknowledge the referral source. Document "self-referred" only after asking about prior contacts with GPs, traditional practitioners, or other psychiatric services.
Where the patient came from to reach psychiatric services. The referral source shapes the expectations the patient brings to the interview and is itself clinically informative.
Document any prior contacts with other practitioners, the pathway to psychiatric care, and any relevant context about the referral (e.g., "referred after failed faith-healing attempt").
A13
Dates
Audit trail for first contact, admission, and current assessment
Document the date of first psychiatric contact (or of the current episode's first contact, if the patient has prior history), the date of current admission/referral, and the date the current case file is being written. All three dates are needed for audit trail.
First contact for the current episode. If the patient has prior psychiatric history, document the date of first contact for the current episode separately.
The date the patient was admitted or referred for the current episode of care.
The date this case file is being written. Defaults to today's date.
Sample Phrasings & Transitions (§G)
G1. Verification of demographic slip
"Before we begin, let me just confirm a few details. I have you as [age] years old, [marital status], working as [occupation], living in [residence], and today accompanied by [informant]. Is all of that correct?"
G2. Addressing the patient
"How would you like me to address you, by your first name, or would you prefer something more formal?"
G3. Informant verification
"[To the informant] And can I just check, how long have you known [patient]? How often do you see her these days? And are you comfortable telling me about what's been going on?"
G4. Gender identity (when relevant)
"I'd like to ask how you identify, I ask everyone, and I want to make sure I'm addressing you correctly. What pronouns do you use?"
G5. Language
"Which language would you be most comfortable speaking in today? Kannada, Hindi, English, or something else? We can switch as we go."
G6. Transition to chief complaints
"Thank you, that gives me the basics. Now I'd like to hear, in your own words, what has brought you to see us today. Take your time."

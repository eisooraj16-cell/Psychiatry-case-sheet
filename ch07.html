A1Antenatal, Perinatal & Neonatal
▼
Clinical Instruction
  • Why it matters: Perinatal complications (prolonged labour, hypoxia, low birth weight, winter birth) are over-represented in schizophrenia. Obstetric history shapes cognitive reserve and neurodevelopmental risk.
  • Ask: The mother or oldest available informant. The patient cannot remember her own perinatal course.
  • Document: If unknowable, write exactly: "Birth and early development not known to the available informants." Do not invent.
  • Pitfall: Documenting antenatal history in a 45-year-old without naming an informant implies fabrication.
Clinical Anchor In adult Indian practice, A1 is very frequently unknowable. The honest documentation is "Birth and early development not known to the available informants." Examiners do not penalise honest absence; they penalise confabulated detail.
Sample phrasing: "I need to ask about your birth and early childhood. Do you have your mother or an older relative who was around at that time?"
If no informant: "Sometimes people cannot tell me much about early childhood — that is alright."

A2Early Childhood, Temperament & Trait/State Boundary
▼
Clinical Instruction
  • Why it matters: Behaviourally inhibited infants carry elevated risk of later anxiety, social phobia, and depression. Highly impulsive infants carry risk of conduct disorder and ADHD.
  • How it shifts differential: Temperament sits on the boundary between developmental event (Ch 07) and stable trait (Ch 08). Describe as developmental fact here; stable adult trait belongs in Chapter 08.
  • Document: Goodness-of-fit (match between temperament and parental environment) — it modifies risk more than either alone.
Rate each on a scale if useful: high / low / not known. Note that behaviourally inhibited temperament (high fearful distress, withdrawal from novelty) is the high-risk subtype for anxiety disorders.
Describe parental sensitivity, scaffolding vs critical/demanding style. Note the match between child's temperament and parenting received.
Key concept: A behaviourally inhibited child with a sensitive, scaffolding parent does well; the same child with a critical, demanding parent develops anxiety. Note this in the formulation — it matters more than either trait or environment alone.
Describe early childhood environment, key relationships, any notable events, separation, moves, caregiving arrangements

A4Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs)
▼
Clinical Instruction
  • Why it matters: ACEs are the single highest-yield item in adult psychiatric personal history. ACE count ≥4 = substantially elevated lifetime risk for depression, suicide attempt, SUD, severe obesity, IHD, COPD, early death.
  • How it shifts differential: Childhood sexual abuse is over-represented in dissociative/FND, BPD, binge-purge AN, PTSD. Witnessed paternal alcohol + maternal beating is the highest-yield pair in Indian alcohol SUD.
  • How to ask: This is a process, not a checklist. Frame → normalise → sequence from less to more sensitive → pause and acknowledge.
  • Pitfall: Reciting the ten items as questions in order will produce refusals. The framing in §C4 is the difference.
Exam Pearl In an Indian adult case, the highest-yield ACE items are: (i) parental loss (death, abandonment) before age 10; (ii) witnessed paternal alcohol use with maternal beating; (iii) sexual abuse (almost always intrafamilial, rarely volunteered); (iv) emotional invalidation / harsh discipline normalised as parenting; (v) early child labour or sent away to relatives for "discipline". Screen all five explicitly. None will be volunteered.
Frame: "Many people who come to see me have had difficult things happen in their childhood, things they have rarely, if ever, told anyone. I am going to ask you about a few of these. You don't have to answer anything you don't want to."

Normalise: "Many people who come to see me" — locates the patient inside a community of people who have answered before.

Sequence: Parental separation → household substance use / mental illness → physical discipline / emotional invalidation → witnessed violence → sexual abuse (last).

Acknowledge: After a disclosure, pause. "Thank you for telling me, that must have been very hard." Do not immediately move to the next question.

Neglect (2 items)
Household Dysfunction (5 items)
Document the patient's words, your observations during disclosure, any emotional response, and the context of each endorsed ACE
A5Schooling
▼
Clinical Instruction
  • Why it matters: Academic trajectory is often more diagnostic than absolute level. A decline in late adolescence (80% in class 10 → 50% in class 12 → failed first-year college) is the single most common premorbid signature of first-episode psychosis.
  • How to document: Plot the trajectory against the symptom timeline. Always ask for grades at 10th, 12th, graduation.
  • Pitfall: Recording only the highest qualification without the trajectory loses the diagnostic signal.
Clinical Anchor A declining academic trajectory in late adolescence, the bright child who was scoring 80% until class 10 and then dropped to 50% in class 12 and then failed first-year college, is the single most common premorbid signature of first-episode psychosis. Always plot the academic trajectory against the symptom timeline.

A6Occupational History
▼
Clinical Instruction
  • Why it matters: Frequent job changes, terminations, occupational drift downward, or sudden collapse suggest mood instability, impulsivity (BPD/ADHD), substance use, prodromal psychosis, or intolerant personality structure.
  • How to document: Chronological list with duration, reason for each change, level of responsibility. For homemakers: assess household role, satisfaction, autonomy, stressors equivalently.
  • High-yield: In BP-I, look for high achievement punctuated by episodic collapses corresponding to episodes.

A7Menstrual & Reproductive History (Women)
▼
Clinical Instruction
  • Why it matters: Reproductive transitions are independent psychiatric vulnerability windows. Postpartum psychosis even decades earlier reframes the entire case as bipolar spectrum with ~30–50% recurrence risk.
  • How to document: Order: age at menarche → current cycle → premenstrual symptoms → LMP → obstetric history → perinatal psychiatric episodes → contraception.
  • Pitfall: Missing this section entirely in women, or omitting the postpartum psychosis screen.
  • Screen: "After any of your deliveries, did you become unwell with confusion, severe sleep loss, hearing voices, or feeling that something bad was about to happen?"
Exam Pearl A history of postpartum psychosis carries a recurrence risk of approximately 30–50% with the next pregnancy and the great majority of these women have an underlying bipolar diathesis even if the postpartum episode was the first lifetime episode. This is one of the most reliably reproduced findings in perinatal psychiatry.

A8Sexual History
▼
Clinical Instruction
  • Why it matters: High-yield in mood disorders (libido follows mood), psychosis (hypersexuality in mania, asexual withdrawal in negative-symptom schizophrenia), OCD (sexual obsessions, scrupulosity), BPD (impulsive chaotic sex), trauma-related disorders.

A10Substance Use Trajectory (Developmental View)
▼
Clinical Instruction
  • Why it matters: For SUD index cases, HOPI carries the dependency syndrome; personal history carries the developmental walk that produced the pattern. For non-SUD cases, background exposure may still be relevant (alcohol as depressive coping, cannabis as psychosis precipitant).
  • How to document: Six-question walk for each substance. Sequence interview: tobacco (least stigmatised) → alcohol → cannabis → prescribed sedatives → stigmatised substances.
  • Pitfall: Bullet-listing substance use without the developmental trajectory. The six-question walk differentiates a casual history from a viva-ready one.
Clinical Anchor Tobacco is the most-missed substance in psychiatric histories and the most relevant prognostically — it is the highest preventable contributor to the mortality gap in patients with serious mental illness. Always ask, separately for smoked and chewed forms.

BWhy Personal History Matters
▼
Four Jobs of Personal History in the Case
  • 1. Developmental: Establishes baseline trajectory against which the index illness is measured. Without it, the case is a snapshot; with it, the case is a film.
  • 2. Aetiological: Surfaces predisposing and precipitating factors for the 4P × bio-psycho-social grid (Chapter 12 §F). ACEs, perinatal complications, conduct trajectory, substance trajectory, marital trajectory, perinatal psychiatric history.
  • 3. Risk-stratifying: Childhood adversity, prior trauma, prior self-harm, prior loss, conduct-to-ASPD trajectory change short-term and lifetime risk estimates.
  • 4. Therapeutic: Schema-aware formulation, attachment-informed therapy choice, family work decisions, brief crisis intervention vs long-term work all rest on this chapter.
How does this patient's personal history serve each of the four jobs? What does it add to formulation, risk, and treatment planning?
CHow to Elicit (General Adult Interview)
▼
C1. Framing the Section

"I'd like to take some time now to understand who you were before any of this began. We'll walk through your life from the beginning. Some of the things I ask will feel personal, please tell me if anything is uncomfortable, and we can come back to it later. Nothing you say here will leave this room without your knowledge."

Four-part framing: the shift, the chronology, the consent to pause, the confidentiality.

C2. Chronological Walk vs Funnelled Approach
Patient stable and articulate = chronological. Acutely unwell, A&E referral, crisis = funnelled. Obvious developmental antecedent the patient is willing to discuss first = funnelled.
C3. Informant vs Patient
First 4 epochs (A1-A4): require informant. Middle epochs (A5-A9): patient-led with corroboration. Last epochs (A10-A12): mixed. Document who was used and discrepancies.

DDisorder-Specific Tailoring
▼
Clinical Instruction

These are worked viva-prose templates for the developmental ground typical of each disorder. They are not index-episode descriptions (those live in Chapter 03 HOPI §D). Use these to guide your elicitation priorities.

1. Schizophrenia / First-Episode Psychosis

Look for: quiet/shy temperament, mild social difficulties in middle childhood, academic decline in late adolescence (the single most common premorbid signature), brief disrupted occupational history, minimal sexual/marital history, cannabis screen, religious help-seeking pre-presentation.

Document the specific elements from this patient's history that match or refute the FEP developmental signature.

2. Mania / Bipolar I Disorder

Look for: high premorbid functioning, "bright one" of family, academically successful, socially extroverted, occupational episodic collapses with brilliant productivity in between, eventful marital history (love marriages, impulsive, higher divorce), perinatal psychiatric history in women is the single most important specific item.

Document hypomanic temperament from late teens, occupational pattern, perinatal history if applicable.

3. Bipolar Depression (BD-I or BD-II Depressive Episode)

Look for: same ground as mania case PLUS the prior unrecognised hypomania probe: "Has there ever been a period when you felt unusually high, energetic, needed less sleep, talked more, did more?" A positive answer reframes unipolar depression as bipolar II.

Patient's exact words. When? Duration? What did family say?

4. Major Depressive Disorder

Look for: childhood loss (especially maternal <11), ACEs (emotional neglect, parental separation), adolescent dysthymic spell described as "always quiet/sensitive", occupational stress-related decompensations anchored to losses, dependent/conflict-avoidant marital pattern, substance self-medication, perinatal psychiatric history in women.

Document childhood loss, ACE load, adolescent mood spell, occupational anchors, marital pattern.

5. Substance Use Disorder, Alcohol

Look for: witnessed paternal alcohol + maternal beating (highest-yield ACE pair in Indian alcohol cases), first exposure 12–16, first regular use late adolescence/early 20s, escalation mid-20s to 30s anchored to stress/separation, marital deterioration paralleling use, IPV as perpetrator under influence, occupational downward drift, traffic/assault forensic history.

Document the specific trajectory elements for this patient.

6. Anxiety / Panic Disorder

Look for: behaviourally inhibited childhood, separation anxiety, school refusal, conscientious-overachiever structure, "good child" who never gave trouble, onset late 20s–30s when pressures exceed coping capacity, stable occupation with chronic anticipatory anxiety, preserved marital history, benzodiazepine use often started by non-psychiatrist physician.

Document temperamental ground, school refusal episodes, occupational stability, benzo history.

7. OCD and Related Disorders

Look for: childhood compulsions/rituals not recognised as abnormal (counting, touching, ordering, refusing cracks, fixed repetitions, scrupulous handwashing), conscientious/perfectionist/rule-bound temperament, preserved schooling through compensatory effort, religious/moral scrupulosity (common in Indian OCD), long pre-presentation phase (mean 7–10 years internationally, longer in India).

Document childhood rituals, scrupulosity, religious preoccupations, duration before presentation.

8. PTSD and Trauma-Related Disorders

Adult trauma = document pre-trauma baseline, prior coping, prior psychiatric history, support structure. Childhood trauma = the personal history is the trauma history; complex PTSD presentation with repeated adversity. Childhood sexual abuse is the most under-elicited and most load-bearing item. Substance self-medication (alcohol, benzodiazepines). Marital pattern of avoidance OR repetition of harmful relationships.

Document trauma type, age, single vs repeated, pre-trauma baseline, coping, support, substance use, marital pattern.

9. Eating Disorder (Anorexia Nervosa, Restricting Type)

Look for: early feeding difficulties, "fussy eater", perfectionist/rigid/conscientious/high-achieving temperament, preserved or excellent schooling, peri-pubertal body image emergence, remembered weight comment from relative/peer as trigger, concurrent menarche, cultural pro-thin/pro-fair attitudes, weight teasing (minimised as harmless), subculture risks (classical dance, modelling, athletics), secondary amenorrhoea.

Document feeding history, body image emergence, triggers, subculture exposure, menstrual impact.

10. Dissociative Disorder / Functional Neurological Disorder

Look for: highest ACE rates in the entire compendium (sexual abuse, neglect, household dysfunction), alexithymic temperament, remembered "model" (relative with stroke/seizure/chronic functional condition), variable schooling, accumulating stressors in year before presentation, IPV/sexual coercion not previously disclosed, trauma history is the most important item.

Document ACE load, alexithymia, remembered model, stressor accumulation, undisclosed IPV.

11. Personality Disorder (BPD-Emphasised)

Look for: attachment disruption, emotional neglect and invalidation (universal), physical abuse (common), childhood sexual abuse (40–70% internationally), high emotional sensitivity/reactivity/slow return to baseline, difficult temperament full cluster, middle childhood behavioural difficulties and peer rejection, adolescence: identity disturbance, relational instability, first self-harm 12–16, substance/sexual experimentation, intense short-lived relationships. Occupational instability, marital pattern of idealisation-devaluation, repeated separations, bidirectional IPV.

Document the developmental trajectory of attachment disruption, emotion dysregulation, identity disturbance, relational instability. This IS the diagnosis.

11b. Antisocial Personality Disorder / Conduct Disorder Trajectory

Look for: witnessed paternal violence, harsh inconsistent discipline, emotional neglect (universal), childhood-onset conduct symptoms (before 10 = worse prognosis), aggression, cruelty, fire-setting, theft, truancy, running away, poor schooling, multiple suspensions/expulsions, dropout by class 8/9, adolescent delinquent peer affiliation, first arrests 14–17, first incarceration late adolescence, substance co-onset, adult multiple arrests across categories, failed parole, unstable occupation, IPV as perpetrator (near-universal), heavy early substance use. The personal history IS the diagnosis.

Document the full forensic arc. Without it, the diagnosis cannot be made.

12. Neurocognitive (Delirium vs Dementia)

Delirium: pre-illness functional/cognitive level, ADL independence, current comorbidities/medications. Dementia: educational level (strongest predictor of cognitive reserve), occupational complexity (skilled/professional > unskilled), social engagement, trajectory of decline against baseline. A retired schoolteacher with 12 years schooling who is now disoriented is a different case from a retired manual labourer with no formal education. Brief marital/sexual/substance unless directly relevant. Document religious/AYUSH help-seeking (accounts for long untreated duration).

Document cognitive reserve explicitly: education, occupational complexity, trajectory of decline, AYUSH trajectory.
EPitfalls & Exam Traps
▼
Checklist: Did you avoid these?
  • Skipping or rushing sexual history under cultural discomfort. Corrective: 5P framework with explicit confidentiality framing (§C5).
  • Treating ACE elicitation as a checklist. Corrective: The §C4 framing is the difference.
  • Failing to take an informant history for early epochs. Corrective: Document honestly when unavailable.
  • Confabulating where data is missing. Corrective: "Birth and early development not known to available informants."
  • Confusing premorbid personality (Ch 08, traits) with personal history (Ch 07, events). Events here, traits next door.
  • Bullet-listing substance use without the developmental trajectory. Corrective: Six-question walk in §A10.
  • Missing menstrual and reproductive history in women. Postpartum psychosis decades earlier reframes the case.
  • Not connecting personal history findings forward to the 4P formulation grid (Ch 12 §F). If findings do not appear in formulation, they were elicited for nothing.
  • Rehashing HOPI substance content for an SUD index case instead of the developmental view. HOPI = dependency syndrome; Personal History = developmental walk.
  • Not screening for conduct disorder antecedent in adult antisocial behaviour. Adult ASPD requires childhood-onset CD evidence.
Document your deliberate strategies to avoid each pitfall. What framing did you use? What did you check?
e.g. Why is temperament in A2 and not Ch 08? Why did you document absence of antenatal data this way?

Quick Reference: Sample Phrasings

▾

Opening Prompt (H1)

“What has brought you to see us today? Please tell me in your own words, and take your time.”

Three Alternatives (H2)

“What do your family members say is the problem?”
“What has changed for you recently that made you come here today?”
“What do you think should be different, if anything could be better, what would it be?”

Duration Anchoring (H3)

“When did you first notice this? Can you give me a rough idea, weeks, months, years? And has it been the same all the way through, or has it got worse at some point?”

Chronological Walk-Forward (H4)

“Of the things you’ve just told me, which one started first? When did that begin? And after that, what came next?”

Informant Parallel (H5)

“I’d like to ask [informant] the same question now, to hear things from a different perspective. [To informant] What do you think has been going on? What are you most worried about?”

Transition to HOPI (H6)

“Thank you. I’d like now to hear more about each of these, starting with the one you said came first. Can you tell me about that in more detail, what was the first thing you noticed, and what happened next?”

A1 Patient Verbatim Complaints

▾

Why it matters

  • The verbatim record is the foundation of every downstream section. Translation into psychiatric vocabulary happens in formulation (Chapter 12), not here.
  • Indian vernacular carries clinical information that no psychiatric translation preserves.

How to document

  • Record exactly what the patient said, in her own language register.
  • Preserve vernacular terms with a brief parenthetical gloss if needed.
  • The patient’s denial or silence is itself a clinical finding — document it verbatim.

Pitfalls

  • Never translate “bejaar” into “depression” at this stage.
  • Do not skip the patient’s account even in severe cases.
Patient Complaint Clusters
Vernacular Capture

Keep the patient’s exact words. If she says “I cannot sleep and my head is heavy”, that is what the file records — not “insomnia and headache”.

Patient Denies Complaints

Document the denial itself: “Patient denies any complaints; states ‘I am better than ever’”. This is clinical data, not an omission.

A2 Informant Verbatim Complaints

▾

Why it matters

  • The informant’s parallel list is not optional when an informant is present.
  • The divergence between patient and informant accounts is itself a diagnostic instrument (see Section D).
  • Do not merge the two lists — preserve both as parallel records.

How to elicit

  • Deliver the same open question to the informant separately.
  • Ideal intake: interview informant together for opening, then separately if the patient’s account may be constrained.
Informant Complaint Clusters
Informant Opener (H5)
“I’d like to ask [informant] the same question now, to hear things from a different perspective. [To informant] What do you think has been going on? What are you most worried about?”
When Patient Cannot Give Account

In mania, psychosis with limited insight, severe depression, or dementia — the informant becomes the primary source. The patient’s account (or denial) is still documented as a separate finding.

A3 Chronological Order

▾

Why it matters

  • The earliest chief complaint is the anchor for the entire HOPI narrative (Chapter 03 §C4).
  • Without a clearly identified earliest complaint, HOPI chronology collapses.
  • The patient reflexively starts with what is most distressing today; your job is to reorder.

How to elicit

  • After the initial list, walk the patient backward: “Of these, which one started first?”
  • Then walk forward: “And after that started, what came next?”

A4 Duration of Each Complaint

▾

Why it matters

  • A complaint without a duration is a symptom without a timeline and is not usable in HOPI.
  • The “since X, increased since Y” format captures both chronicity and recent escalation.

Convention

  • Stable complaint: “since 2 years”
  • Escalated complaint: “since 2 years, increased since 6 months”
Duration Tagging
Duration Anchoring Probe (H3)
“When did you first notice that? Can you give me a rough idea, weeks, months, years? And has it been the same all the way through, or has it got worse at some point?”

A5 Mode of Onset

▾

Why it matters

  • Onset acuity is a major differential discriminator (delirium vs dementia, mania vs depression).
  • Acute = hours to days; Subacute = days to weeks; Insidious = weeks to months to years.

A6 Course

▾

Why it matters

  • Temporal pattern helps distinguish episodic disorders (bipolar, panic) from continuous disorders (dysthymia, GAD).
  • Progressive course points toward neurocognitive or neurodegenerative processes.

A7 Functional Impairment

▾

Why it matters

  • Brief summary only here. Detailed impact on occupation, relationships, self-care, and risk belongs to HOPI (Chapter 03).
  • Examples: “stopped going to work”, “stopped cooking”, “stopped leaving the house”.

Pitfall

  • Over-documenting functional impact at the CC stage collapses the structure of the case file.

D Discrepancy as Diagnostic Instrument

▾

Why it matters

  • The direction of the discrepancy maps onto specific diagnostic categories.
  • The divergence is a finding, not a nuisance — do not merge the two lists into one.

How to document

  • Document the discrepancy explicitly: “Patient reports ‘...’; Informant reports ‘...’”.
  • Both lists go into the case file as two parallel records.
Characteristic Discrepancy Pattern
Discrepancy Analysis (free text)
Document the explicit discrepancy here. Example format: “Patient reports ‘I am fine, I don’t know why I am here’; Informant reports four nights without sleep, pressured speech, indiscriminate spending...”

E Documentation Convention / Final Preview

▾

Standard Format (OCD Bharti style)

  • Grouped by theme. Related complaints into a single line.
  • Duration at the end of each group: “since X, increased since Y”.
  • Verbatim where possible.
  • Patient complaints first; informant block second where it differs.

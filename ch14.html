Exam Pearl In KMCRI viva, Section 89 is the highest-yield single piece of MHCA knowledge. If the case is acute mania, severe psychosis, severe depression with active suicidal intent, or neurocognitive disorder with risk and incapacity — name it.

💊 Drug Defence — 6 Criterion Framework

▼
  1. Indication — the diagnosis justifies the drug class
  2. Evidence — IPS-CPG citation level, or international guideline
  3. Patient fit — comorbidity, age, sex, pregnancy, organ function, prior response, family response history
  4. Safety profile — side effects relevant to this patient
  5. Accessibility & affordability — Indian-context cost and availability
  6. Exit criteria — review point, response criteria, switch or escalation plan
Clinical Anchor A candidate who can name five drugs but cannot say which one for this patient is failing. A candidate who names one drug, defends it on six criteria, and names one alternative is passing. Always explain why this drug for this patient.

⚠️ Pitfalls & Exam Traps

▼
  • Forgetting safety first. Every plan begins with current risk.
  • Polypharmacy without indication. Every drug needs defended indication, dose, duration, exit criterion.
  • Starting an antidepressant in untreated bipolar. IPS-CPG 2026: antidepressant monotherapy is NOT recommended in bipolar depression.
  • Benzodiazepines in BPD, delirium, dementia, PTSD, anorexia nervosa, and substance use (other than alcohol/BZD withdrawal).
  • Not naming drug, dose, duration, and monitoring plan. "I would start an SSRI" is not an answer.
  • Mixing up "supportive" with "specific" psychotherapy. Name the modality, sessions, and content.
  • Ignoring the family in an Indian case. Family meeting in first 24 hours is central.
  • Forgetting the cost dimension. Lurasidone, vortioxetine, aripiprazole LAI are unaffordable for most Indian patients.
  • Not specifying monitoring. Each drug carries a named monitoring plan with frequencies.
  • Forgetting MHCA where it applies. Acute mania, severe psychosis, severe depression with active SI, NCD with risk and incapacity: name Section 89.
  • "Refer to psychologist" is never adequate. Name modality, goal, number of sessions, and phase.
  • Forgetting the discharge plan. Day one of admission is day one of discharge planning.
  • Valproate in women of reproductive age without MHRA 2024 / IPS-CPG 2026 safeguards documented.
  • Olanzapine in AN without acknowledging it is off-label and weakly evidenced. Frame as adjunct, never as treatment.
  • Generic plans. "CBT, SSRI, family work, follow-up" applied to two different patients is a fail. Reference the patient's specific features.

▼

PTSD

▼
Exam Pearl Benzodiazepines are NOT recommended in PTSD. They impair fear-extinction learning, worsen long-term outcomes, and increase substance-use disorder risk (IPS-CPG 2017 explicit recommendation against).
Immediate priorities Suicide risk high with comorbid depression and substance use. C-SSRS at every visit. Screen for comorbid depression, alcohol use, dissociation, sleep disturbance. Eating Disorder — Anorexia Nervosa (Restricting Type)

▼
Immediate priorities AN carries the highest mortality of any psychiatric illness. Medical risk against MARSIPAN. Refeeding plan that avoids refeeding syndrome. Start low (10–20 kcal/kg/day), slow (increase by 200–300 kcal/day), thiamine first, phosphate replacement, daily electrolytes × first week, ECG monitoring. Compulsory admission (Sec 89) if BMI <13 or any red flag and patient refuses voluntary admission.
Immediate Actions & Medical Risk
Start 10–20 kcal/kg/day, increase by 200–300 kcal/day. Thiamine first. Phosphate replacement. Daily electrolytes × first week. ECG monitoring. Phosphate-Sandoz 1–2 tablets TDS prophylactically if phosphate low-normal.
Pharmacotherapy (Adjuncts Only)
Under MHCA with explicit legal documentation and ethics review if medical risk high and oral refeeding failed...
Psychological, Social, Disposition & Prognosis
Family meeting (family is the treatment system in FBT), school/college liaison, peer support, carer support group...
Medical ward/ICU for acute medical instability (MARSIPAN red flags). Specialist eating-disorder unit where available. Admit Sec 89 for BMI <13, red flags, or refusal with incapacity. OPD FBT/CBT-E for stable patients with BMI >15 and motivation. Transition: weekly weight in early treatment → biweekly later → monthly medical review until weight-restored → long-term psychiatric follow-up ≥2 years post weight restoration.
Good: adolescent onset, short duration <3 years, restricting subtype, supportive family for FBT, no comorbid psychiatric illness, no premorbid obesity, higher minimum BMI, no purging, response to weight-restoration in first 4 weeks, good therapeutic alliance, motivation... D10

Dissociative / Functional Neurological Disorder

▼
Immediate priorities Avoid iatrogenic harm from over-investigation, repeated invasive procedures, contradictory labels. Positive diagnosis (not diagnosis of exclusion): Hoover's sign, tremor entrainment, dissociative-seizure semiology. Avoid "not real", "in your head", "psychosomatic" — these damage alliance and are a common viva failure point. Screen comorbid depression, anxiety, trauma, chronic pain. Limit further investigations once diagnosis confirmed.
Immediate Actions & Diagnosis
'Your nervous system has the wiring intact but the software is mis-firing. This is real, it is not under your conscious control, and it is treatable.'
Pharmacotherapy (Comorbid Conditions Only)
Clinical Anchor Avoid benzodiazepines — they worsen dissociation and create dependence.
Psychological, Social, Disposition & Prognosis
Family psychoeducation about FND as real and treatable, return-to-work planning, address maintaining psychosocial stressors, MDT review (psychiatry, neurology, physiotherapy)...
OPD throughout. Avoid admission wherever possible as it may reinforce sick role. Specialist neuropsychiatric/rehabilitation unit for severe motor FND unresponsive to outpatient treatment. Transition: weekly ×1 month → monthly.
Good: acute onset, identifiable acute stressor, short duration <6 months, early diagnosis with positive explanation, good alliance, no comorbid depression/anxiety, no prior FND, no secondary gain, engagement with physiotherapy/psychotherapy, intact premorbid functioning, supportive family that accepts diagnosis... Bad: insidious/fluctuating onset, no identifiable stressor, long duration >1 year, delayed diagnosis with multiple negative investigations, motor deficits worse than sensory/seizure subtypes, comorbid chronic pain/fatigue, comorbid depression/anxiety, secondary gain, prior FND episodes, dependent personality features, family that rejects diagnosis, iatrogenic harm from over-investigation, dissociative seizures at high frequency...
D11

Personality Disorder — BPD Emphasised

▼
Immediate priorities BPD has 5–10% lifetime suicide mortality. Brief, planned crisis intervention — NOT a long admission (iatrogenic). Written crisis plan with named contacts. Explicit plan to avoid polypharmacy. No new drug starts during an acute crisis. Consistent plan across providers (inconsistency is iatrogenic).
Immediate Actions
Pharmacotherapy (Targeted, Time-Limited, Minimal)
Clinical Anchor NICE CG78 is explicit: drug treatment should NOT be used specifically for BPD or for individual BPD symptoms/behaviours. Where used at all, targets are co-occurring conditions. Prescribing principle: minimum dose, minimum duration, single agent. Benzodiazepines are contraindicated — they disinhibit, worsen impulsivity, increase self-harm, and are habit-forming.
Every drug added needs an explicit exit criterion. Document review dates and taper plans...
Psychological, Social, Disposition & Prognosis
Family psychoeducation about BPD as real and treatable (not bad behaviour), workplace adjustments, peer support (online BPD support groups, carer groups)...
OPD as default. Brief planned admission (<72 hours) only for acute risk that cannot be safely contained outpatient, with planned discharge date set at admission. Avoid long admissions and repeated brief admissions (iatrogenic). Admit Sec 89 only in exceptional crisis with confirmed incapacity. Transition: weekly individual contact during first year of any structured psychotherapy, long-term continuity with one primary clinician.
Good: engagement with structured psychotherapy, continuity with one primary clinician, no comorbid substance use/antisocial features, intact occupational functioning, supportive long-term relationship, no childhood sexual abuse, age (~85% remit by age 50 per McLean), avoidance of polypharmacy, avoidance of long admissions, motivation for change... Bad: comorbid substance use, comorbid antisocial personality features, severe childhood trauma (especially repeated sexual abuse), repeated self-harm/suicide attempts, multiple short hospitalisations (iatrogenic), long admissions (iatrogenic), polypharmacy, frequent therapist changes, family rejection, occupational disability, severe affective instability, severe identity disturbance, chronic suicidality, comorbid eating disorder...

Neurocognitive Disorder — Delirium & Dementia

▼
DELIRIUM
Immediate priorities Falls prevention, identification and treatment of cause, symptomatic control with lowest effective dose of non-benzodiazepine agent. Gentle reorientation, family at bedside, photograph of family, hearing aid and glasses returned. Stop precipitating drugs: opioids, anticholinergics, benzodiazepines, steroids, antihistamines.
Exam Pearl Benzodiazepines are contraindicated in delirium EXCEPT when cause is alcohol or benzodiazepine withdrawal, in which case lorazepam is the treatment.
DEMENTIA (Alzheimer's Pattern, Mild-to-Moderate)
Immediate priorities Wandering risk, kitchen safety, driving cessation, financial protection, falls prevention, capacity assessment for treatment decisions, advance care planning, MHCA Section 5 advance directive introduced early.
5 mg/day → 10 mg BD over 4 weeks, target 20 mg/day; monitor renal function baseline and 6-monthly; ~₹190–285/strip
Exam Pearl Antipsychotics carry a black-box warning for mortality in dementia (cerebrovascular events and all-cause mortality). Time-limit use, review at 6 weeks, taper where possible.
Psychological, Social, Disposition & Prognosis
Caregiver psychoeducation (caregiver burden is strongest predictor of nursing-home placement), respite care and ARDSI linkage, driving cessation discussion, financial protection (power of attorney, joint account), advance care planning and end-of-life discussion...
Delirium: manage on medical/surgical ward where underlying cause treated, psychiatry consultation-liaison; specialist Older Adults Mental Health unit if available. Dementia: home-based care with caregiver support default in Indian context; day-care for moderate stages; specialist dementia care home for severe stages where home care unsustainable. Admit Sec 89 for severe BPSD with risk and incapacity. Follow-up: cognitive reassessment 4–6 weeks post-delirium discharge; 6-monthly cognitive review in dementia (MMSE/HMSE); quarterly caregiver review.
Delirium good: identifiable reversible cause, prompt treatment, short duration <1 week, no premorbid cognitive impairment, younger age, no comorbidity, supportive family at bedside... Delirium bad: multiple irreversible causes, delayed treatment, long duration, pre-existing cognitive impairment, advanced age, multiple comorbidities, hypoactive/mixed subtype, ICU-acquired, mechanical ventilation, benzodiazepine sedation... Dementia good: later age at onset, slower progression, intact insight early, supportive caregiver, early cholinesterase inhibitor, good vascular risk factor control, absence of BPSD/comorbid depression, advance care planning in place... Dementia bad: early-onset <65, rapid decline, frontotemporal subtype, severe BPSD, comorbid depression, poor caregiver support, caregiver burnout, multiple medical comorbidities, recurrent delirium episodes...

Prognosis Across Disorders — Consolidated

▼
How to document Never give a flat "the prognosis is good" or "the prognosis is poor". The viva-grade answer is always qualified: which factors are favourable, which are unfavourable, what is the net statement, and what would change the trajectory most.
Exam Pearl For every disorder the viva expects a qualified net statement. The format is: "good factors are X, Y, Z, bad factors are A, B, C, the net in this patient is [guarded / cautiously favourable / poor], and the single change that would most improve the trajectory is [specific intervention]."
In this patient, the prognosis is [guarded/cautiously favourable/poor] because...
'In this patient, the prognosis is guarded but cautiously favourable because A, B, and C, despite D and E.'

Sample Stems (Verbatim Viva Openings)

▼
How to document These are canonical opening sentences for the viva. Customise each for your patient by inserting the diagnosis, drug names, doses, and patient-specific features. Practise saying them aloud until they are automatic.
My management of this patient with [diagnosis] would proceed in five domains. I would address safety first, then biological treatment, then psychological intervention, then social and family work, and finally disposition and follow-up. Across three time phases, immediate, short-term, and long-term.
The most pressing concern in this case is [specific risk]. I would address this immediately by [specific action]. The rationale is [patient-specific feature].
I would start [drug] at [dose], titrating to [target dose] over [duration]. The indication is [diagnosis]. The IPS-CPG 2026 lists this as a [first-line/second-line] option. The reason I prefer this over [alternative] in this patient is [specific feature]. I would monitor [named investigation] at [frequency] and review response at [week]. If response is inadequate by [week], I would [specific next step].
I would convene a family meeting within 24 hours of admission. Present would be the patient where capacity allows, the primary caregiver, and the key family decision-maker. The agenda would cover the diagnosis in lay language, the treatment plan with rationale, the expected timeline of response, side effects to watch for, early warning signs of relapse, the family's specific role, and what to do in a crisis.
This patient meets criteria for supported admission under MHCA Section 89 because they have shown an inability to care for themselves to a degree that places them at risk, they lack capacity to make the admission decision, and admission is the least restrictive option that ensures safety. The application would be made by [family member] as the nominated representative, and the admission confirmed by two psychiatrists. Mental Health Review Board notification is required if the admission extends beyond 30 days.
Discharge planning began on day one. The discharge package consists of a written prescription with timing and doses, a follow-up appointment within 2 weeks, a crisis plan with three named contacts, a family briefing about relapse-warning signs, and a referral to the local DMHP outreach worker for continuity. The patient leaves with the medication in hand, not a prescription to fill.

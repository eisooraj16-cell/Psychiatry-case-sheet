Pitfalls & Exam Traps

  • Confusing prodrome with premorbid baseline — Most common error. A year of declining social engagement before first psychotic episode is prodrome (HOPI), not premorbid personality.
  • Accepting patient self-report without informant — Patient's account is systematically contaminated by current state. Informant is primary.
  • Listing traits without supporting examples — "Rigid, perfectionist, anxious" without concrete illustration is a list, not a description.
  • Skipping this chapter in adult-onset cases — Even first depression at age 50 has a premorbid personality that matters.
  • Missing cyclothymic/hyperthymic temperament in bipolar — Frame as "was her baseline different from most people?" not "was she ever high or low?"
  • Using trait labels as diagnoses — Schizoid traits are not schizoid PD; anankastic traits are not OCPD. Functional impairment criterion lives in Ch 12.
  • Ignoring age-stratification (<16 temperament vs adult personality) — Different frameworks; candidate must defend choice.
  • Describing temperament without goodness of fit — Temperament alone does not predict outcome; trait-environment interaction does.
  • Treating premorbid as formulaic filler — If it can be ignored without affecting diagnosis or treatment plan, it has not been done correctly.

A NIMHANS Framework
Why this matters: This is the trait chapter — the developmental substrate on which the 4P grid (Ch 12) and treatment plan (Ch 14) rest. The central clinical question: How do we separate the illness from the person?
  • For patients ≥16 years: document across 7 domains (Brown and Fish traditions).
  • For patients <16 years: use temperament frameworks (Thomas & Chess or Rothbart & Bates).
  • The boundary at 16 is a clinical convention — defend your choice in the case file.
A1Social Relations
Diagnostic shift: A lifelong pattern of excessive dependence on one parent then spouse = dependent trait. Lifelong distant minimal-contact relationships = schizoid trait. Three high-yield patterns: lifelong loners, socially anxious children→adults, superficially sociable with no close friendships.
Nature of attachments to parents, siblings, spouse, children. Dependent or autonomous? Compliant or rebellious? Warm, distant, conflicted, or enmeshed?
English sentinel
"How was she with people, family, friends, at work? Did she have close friends or was she more of a loner?"
Hindi
"Logon ke saath uska bartaav kaisa tha, ghar mein, doston ke saath, kaam par? Kya uske kuch kareebi dost the, ya woh akele rehne wali thi?"
Hinglish
"Logon ke saath uska behaviour kaisa tha, family, friends, at work? Kya uske close friends the ya woh zyada loner type thi?"
Number of sustained friendships (not acquaintances), depth, pattern of making/losing friends, ease of new social contact.
Religious, cultural, neighbourhood, professional, hobby group participation. Wider support network = better relapse prevention.
Leadership style, conflict resolution, ambition, stability of employment (see A5), quality/stability of romantic/sexual partnerships (see Ch 07 §A8-A9).
A2Intellectual & Leisure Activities
Diagnostic shift: Decline from rich intellectual life to blank withdrawal = state change (depression, negative-symptom psychosis, neurocognitive decline). Lifelong absence of interests = trait (schizoid, intellectually limited, dysthymic anhedonia).
Trait-state guide: A previously rich intellectual life now withdrawn = state change. A lifelong absence of interests = trait. Record discriminator: "Was she always like this, or is this different from how she used to be?"
A3Mood Disposition
Critical distinction: This is the baseline affective tone — not current mood, not mood during episodes. The mood she was in most of the time across months and years when nothing particular was going on. A lifelong cheerful baseline now flat = state change on normal trait. Lifelong anxious baseline now worse = depressive episode on depressive trait (trait is itself vulnerability). Lifelong short-duration highs and lows = cyclothymic temperament (shifts bipolar spectrum likelihood substantially).
English sentinel
"What was her usual mood like, was she mostly cheerful, mostly quiet, mostly worried, mostly up and down? How did she handle ordinary stress?"
Hindi
"Uski aam taur par mann ki halat kaisi rehti thi, zyadatar khush, zyadatar chup, zyadatar pareshaan, ya oopar-neeche? Woh rozmarra ki pareshaniyan kaise sambhalti thi?"
Hinglish
"Uska usual mood kaisa rehta tha, mostly cheerful, mostly quiet, mostly worried, ya up and down? Ordinary stress ko woh kaise handle karti thi?"
A4Character & Interpersonal Traits
The heart of personality description. This is where personality disorder traits are documented when they exist. Remember: trait descriptions are not diagnoses. The step from trait to PD diagnosis requires functional impairment and is made in Chapter 12 §D11, not here.
  • Anankastic: rigid perfectionism, over-conscientiousness, difficulty delegating.
  • Paranoid: mistrust, guardedness, neutral events interpreted as threatening.
  • Histrionic: dramatic, attention-seeking, emotionally exaggerated expression.
Each trait named should carry at least one example from the informant's account.
English sentinel
"What kind of personality did she have? Was she careful or spontaneous, confident or shy, flexible or set in her ways, trusting or cautious, easily hurt or thick-skinned?"

A5Energy & Initiative
Diagnostic shift: Chronic low energy as lifelong trait = dysthymia substrate / low-energy depressive personality. Chronic high energy + short sleep = hyperthymic temperament (background for bipolar I mania). Episodic productivity interrupted by collapse = cyclothymic/hyperthymic signature. Seasonal affective pattern often emerges here.

Diagnostic shift: Excessive rich fantasy life as lifelong trait = schizotypal traits, dissociative presentations, maladaptive daydreaming. Absent fantasy life with purely pragmatic inner experience = normal variant / alexithymic presentations.

Children & Adolescents (< 16 years) — Temperament
Before adult personality structure has consolidated, temperament is the concept. Biologically based individual difference in emotional reactivity, motor reactivity, and self-regulation observable from infancy. The NIMHANS standard is the Thomas & Chess framework; Rothbart & Bates is the contemporary research refinement.
T&CThomas & Chess — 9 Dimensions & 3 Types
R&BRothbart & Bates — 6-Dimension Refinement
GoFGoodness of Fit
Exam Trap: Describing temperament without describing goodness of fit. Temperament alone does not predict outcome. The trait-environment interaction does. A difficult child with a responsive parent does well; the same child with a depleted parent does not.
B Why It Matters — The Four Jobs
Document the four jobs so the case file makes clear why this chapter carries formulation weight. Each job links forward to later chapters.
How each MSE finding is interpreted against this baseline. Blunted affect in previously vivacious patient = state change. Same blunted affect in lifelong schizoid = trait.
Which traits elevate risk for which conditions in this patient? This becomes the predisposing column of the 4P grid (Ch 12 §F).
Lifelong pervasive inflexible pattern documented here = evidence for any PD diagnostic call made in Ch 12 §D11.
Premorbid high-functioning → higher post-episode baseline. Rehabilitation, return-to-work, occupational re-entry all rest on accurate estimate of pre-illness functional ceiling.
C How to Elicit — Interview Technique
C1Who to Ask — Informant, Informant, Informant
Exam Trap: Taking the patient's self-report at face value. The patient's account of her premorbid self is systematically contaminated by current state. The informant is the standard source; patient self-report is corroborative, not primary.
C2The Anchor Question
The single most important opening move. "Setting aside everything that has happened since she became unwell" = temporal anchor. "How would the people who know her best describe her" = invites characterological response. An articulate informant often delivers 70% of the premorbid assessment in 2 minutes.
C3The Seven-Domain Sweep
After the anchor question, sweep the seven domains with one sentinel question each. Roughly 10 minutes in a routine case. Use the trilingual phrasings in §G or the quick-reference panels above.
C4The Trait-State Discriminator
The workhorse question. Applied serially to every MSE finding that could plausibly be a long-standing trait: flat affect, poor eye contact, perseveration, suspicion, indecision, irritability, anhedonia, social withdrawal, etc. The answer is recorded as "trait" or "state" or "unclear on collateral, further corroboration needed".
C5Childhood & Adolescent Temperament Interview
For patients under 16, or older adolescents where adult framework does not apply. Walk through Thomas & Chess nine dimensions one by one. Conclude with type classification and goodness-of-fit discussion. Goodness of fit is mandatory — temperament without environmental context cannot carry formulation weight.

C6The Prodrome-versus-Premorbid Trap
Exam Pearl: The single most common error is including prodromal features as if they were long-standing baseline. The prodrome is early illness, not premorbid. A year of declining social engagement before first psychotic episode = prodrome (HOPI, Ch 03), NOT premorbid personality.
D Personality Frameworks — Theory
The NIMHANS seven-domain framework is a clinical convention, not a theoretical framework. The candidate must name and use the theoretical frameworks below as lenses over the seven domains.
D1Trait vs State Distinction
D2Thomas & Chess — 9 Dimensions / 3 Types
D3Rothbart & Bates — 6-Dimension Refinement
D4Cloninger 7-Factor Model (TCI)
4 temperament dimensions (genetically anchored, stable from early life): Novelty Seeking, Harm Avoidance, Reward Dependence, Persistence. 3 character dimensions (shaped by learning): Self-directedness, Cooperativeness, Self-transcendence.
D5Big Five (OCEAN)
The contemporary default dimensional model in academic psychology. High neuroticism = single strongest predictor of depression and anxiety disorders. High extraversion = bipolar spectrum. High conscientiousness = protective against substance use and antisocial outcomes.
D6ICD-11 Five Trait Domains
ICD-11 abandoned categorical PD classification and adopted a dimensional model: any PD is first rated for severity (mild/moderate/severe), then described along five trait domains. The Borderline Pattern qualifier is retained as an additional specifier.

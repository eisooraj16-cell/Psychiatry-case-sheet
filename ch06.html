A. NIMHANS Framework — Nine Sub-Sections

A1 Three-Generation Pedigree

▼

💡 Why this matters

  • The pedigree is the visual anchor of the entire chapter — the first thing an examiner looks at.
  • Three generations (patient, parents, grandparents) is standard; extend downward to children if relevant age, upward only for dementia/familial neurodegenerative differentials.
  • Dating (ages, ages at death, ages at onset) transforms the pedigree from a diagram into clinical evidence.

Without dating, the pedigree is flat and examinable as an error. Always date every individual.

Exam Pearl: A flat pedigree without dating is the single most common error examiners catch. No ages, no ages at death, no ages at onset = incomplete pedigree.

Extend downward when patient has children of relevant age. Extend upward only for dementia/familial neurodegenerative differentials.

e.g., Grandfather's age at death unknown — family reports 'very old'...
Describe any affected relatives with diagnosis and age of onset; consanguinity; unusual patterns; cause of death annotations...

A4 Family Psychiatric History

▼

💡 Why this matters — highest-yield sub-section

  • Shifts the differential diagnosis before symptom history is even completed.
  • For each affected 1°, 2°, or 3° relative, document four items: diagnosis (family words → contemporary), age of onset, treatment received, outcome.
  • Suicide must be screened explicitly — never assumed present or absent from spontaneous narrative.

Family vocabulary in India is loose ("nervous breakdown", "tension", "madness"). Map carefully without overclaiming. ECT in a relative = severe illness that families remember.

Clinical Anchor: Indian families often answer "no" to psychiatric illness. Use the expanded probe to convert ~50% of negatives to positives.

Elder brother: diagnosed schizophrenia age 25, treated at NIMHANS, 3 admissions, currently on olanzapine, partial recovery.

Affected Second- & Third-Degree Relatives

Suicide Screen

Exam Pearl: Many family suicides recorded as "fell from roof", "accidental hanging", "consumed something by mistake", "sudden cardiac death" — probe directly.

A5 Family Medical History

▼

💡 Why this matters

  • Conditions in 1° and 2° relatives clinically relevant to psychiatric management.
  • Cardiovascular history (sudden cardiac death) → relevant for QTc-prolonging psychotropics.
  • Diabetes → relevant for second-generation antipsychotics and metabolic syndrome.
  • Thyroid disease → relevant for lithium and depression differential.
  • Epilepsy, dementia, autoimmune → each carries neuropsychiatric overlap.
Father: hypertension diagnosed age 45, on amlodipine. Maternal uncle: sudden cardiac death age 52 — relevant for QTc monitoring if antipsychotics considered.
Mother: hypothyroidism on thyroxine. Maternal grandmother: type 2 diabetes from age 55. Relevant for metabolic monitoring if olanzapine/quetiapine considered.
Paternal grandfather: dementia onset age 72, died age 78. No autopsy. Relevant for neurocognitive workup if patient has memory concerns.
Document type, relative, age at diagnosis, outcome. Relevant for psychosocial context and caregiver burden.

A6 Consanguinity

▼

💡 Why this matters

  • Sufficiently common in many Indian regions (southern India, Muslim communities, endogamous groups) — asked routinely, never assumed absent.
  • Raises suspicion for autosomal recessive conditions including metabolic and neurodegenerative disorders presenting with neuropsychiatric features.
  • Wilson's disease is the highest-yield single example in a young adult presentation.
e.g., Young adult with movement disorder + psychiatric symptoms → Wilson's disease in differential. Consanguinity increases suspicion for AR inheritance.

A7 Family Environment & Expressed Emotion

▼

💡 Why this matters

  • EE is the largest single modifiable predictor of relapse in schizophrenia and substantial in bipolar, depression, eating disorders, and dementia.
  • Document EE state on admission so Chapter 14 family intervention has a baseline to work against.
  • Indian families: high-EE prevalence lower (~24% Chandigarh) than Western (~48–70%), but prognostic effect preserved when present. EOI relatively more prominent; criticism relatively less.

ℹ️ Sample EE elicitation probes (H5)
"How would you describe how things are between you and your [son/daughter/wife/husband] right now? When she is unwell, how do you find yourself reacting? A9 Caregiver Identification & Burden

▼

💡 Why this matters

  • Treatment in Indian psychiatric practice is family treatment more than individual treatment.
  • Primary caregiver often ≠ decision-maker. Caregiver may be daughter-in-law/wife/mother; decision-maker may be son/husband/father.
  • Discharge plan that hands a chronic schizophrenia patient back to a burnt-out caregiver is a relapse plan.
  • Zarit Burden Interview (ZBI) is the standard formal instrument; routine case file uses clinical summary.
e.g., Wife, 32, homemaker, well. Administers medication, accompanies to clinic, monitors symptoms.
e.g., Decision-maker is patient's elder brother (35, engineer, lives abroad). Financial decisions and treatment consent flow through him via phone.

Caregiver Burden Assessment (Clinical Summary)

e.g., Caregiver reports insomnia for 3 months, 5 kg weight loss, frequent headaches.

Higher scores = greater burden. >60 often indicates severe burden.

E3–E4 Cut-Off Conventions & Relapse Data

▼

💡 High-EE cut-offs

  • ≥6 critical comments by a single key relative during interview
  • Any rating of hostility above zero
  • EOI rating ≥3 (on 0–5 scale)
  • Otherwise classified as low EE. Dichotomous high-vs-low is the standard for prognostic literature.

English unless otherwise marked. Substitute local language equivalent where appropriate. These phrasings are designed to convert guarded or negative responses into clinically useful disclosures.

ℹ️ H2. Family psychiatric history — the expanded probe
"Has anyone in your family ever had any kind of nervous trouble? I'm asking broadly — has anyone had a problem with sadness that did not lift, with worry or fear that interfered with daily life, with being unable to sleep for many nights together, with hearing voices that others could not hear, with being suspicious of people without reason, with drinking alcohol heavily, with being taken to a temple or a faith healer for relief, with being treated by a psychiatrist, or with receiving electric shock treatment?"

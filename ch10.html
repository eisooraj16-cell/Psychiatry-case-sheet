Quick Ref: Pitfalls & Exam Traps ▼

Critical Pitfalls from §K

  • Doing only HMF and skipping motor/sensory → "grossly intact" on brief cognitive screen is NOT a neurological exam
  • Missing cerebellar signs in chronic alcohol use → check truncal/gait ataxia, not just finger-nose
  • Documenting "CNS, NAD" without examining → examiners will ask you to demonstrate
  • Missing catatonia screen in stupor/mutism/posturing/agitation → BFCRS takes 2 minutes
  • Missing Wernicke's in confused alcoholic → treat with thiamine FIRST; never give glucose before thiamine
  • Failing to document Hoover's sign & positive FND signs → FND is diagnosed by positive signs, not absence of findings
  • Mistaking dissociative seizures for epileptic seizures → semiology discrimination is high-yield
  • Skipping CN I (olfactory) in frontal/early neurodegeneration presentations
  • Using English cognitive instrument in non-English-literate patient → use HMSE
  • Interpreting positive glabellar tap as Parkinson's without context → Myerson's sign is non-specific
  • Missing AIMS in long-term antipsychotic → screen at intake and 6-monthly

I. Functional Neurological Disorder — Positive Signs

FND diagnosis is made by demonstration of positive bedside signs of functional dysfunction, NOT by exclusion of organic disease.

I1
Hoover's sign
▼

Why this matters

  • Single most useful positive sign in functional leg weakness
  • High specificity, moderate sensitivity
  • Central diagnostic manoeuvre in FND
1. Direct testing: lift affected leg against resistance → weakness. 2. Contralateral hip flexion: lift unaffected leg → normal physiology produces reflex extension of contralateral hip. 3. Hoover finding: affected leg pushes down strongly during contralateral manoeuvre despite inability to lift directly.
